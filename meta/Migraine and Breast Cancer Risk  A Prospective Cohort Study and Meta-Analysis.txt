                  <h2>Abstract</h2>
                  <div id="sec-1" class="subsection">
                     <p id="p-1"><strong>Background:</strong> The evidence for an association between migraine and breast cancer risk is inconclusive. While female sex hormones have been
                        proposed as one underlying mechanism, data on sex hormone levels in migraineurs are sparse.
                     </p>
                  </div>
                  <div id="sec-2" class="subsection">
                     <p id="p-2"><strong>Methods:</strong> We prospectively evaluated the association between migraine and breast cancer risk among 115378 Nurses’ Health Study II participants
                        using Cox proportional hazards models. Differences in endogenous sex hormone levels according to migraine status were assessed
                        among 2034 premenopausal women using linear regression. We performed a meta-analysis of studies investigating the association
                        between migraine and invasive breast cancer risk published through October 2013. All statistical tests were two-sided.
                     </p>
                  </div>
                  <div id="sec-3" class="subsection">
                     <p id="p-3"><strong>Results:</strong> Seventeen thousand six hundred ninety-six women (15.3%) reported a physician’s diagnosis of migraine at baseline. Over 20
                        years of follow-up, 833 in situ and 3091 invasive breast malignancies occurred. Migraine was not associated with total (hazard
                        ratio [HR] = 0.96, 95% confidence interval [CI] = 0.88 to 1.04), in situ (HR = 0.97, 95% CI = 0.82 to 1.15), or invasive breast
                        cancer risk (HR = 0.95, 95% CI = 0.87 to 1.04). Endogenous sex hormone levels did not differ according to migraine status.
                        In the meta-analysis, migraine was associated with a lower risk of breast cancer overall (pooled risk ratio [RR] = 0.84, 95%
                        CI = 0.73 to 0.98). However, this inverse association was apparent only among case-control studies (pooled RR = 0.72, 95%
                        CI = 0.66 to 0.79), and not among cohort studies (pooled RR = 0.98, 95% CI = 0.87 to 1.10).
                     </p>
                  </div>
                  <div id="sec-4" class="subsection">
                     <p id="p-4"><strong>Conclusion:</strong> In this large cohort study, migraine was not associated with breast cancer risk or differences in endogenous sex hormone
                        levels. While case-control studies suggest an inverse association between migraine and breast cancer risk, prospective cohort
                        studies do not support an association in pooled analyses.
                     </p>
                  </div>
               </div>
               <p id="p-5">Migraine, a common primary headache disorder, is a heterogeneous disease primarily affecting women (<a id="xref-ref-1-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-1">1</a>). There is increasing evidence that migraine is linked to diverse comorbidities (<a id="xref-ref-2-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-2">2</a>), and an association between migraine and breast cancer has been proposed. In two large case-control studies, migraineurs
                  had a 26% to 33% lower risk of invasive breast cancer and a prospective cohort study suggested a more modest 11% lower risk
                  (<a id="xref-ref-3-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-3">3–5</a>). However, a recently published large prospective cohort study did not observe an association (<a id="xref-ref-6-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>).
               </p>
               <p id="p-6" class="indent">The mechanisms by which migraine may be associated with breast cancer remain unclear. Since fluctuating estrogen levels may
                  trigger migraine attacks and endogenous hormones are linked to breast cancer (<a id="xref-ref-7-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-7">7–9</a>), it has been hypothesized that pathways involving female sex hormones may play a role in the association (<a id="xref-ref-3-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-3">3–5</a>). Some small clinic-based studies among premenopausal women suggest that migraineurs may have different sex hormone levels,
                  however, the results are inconsistent (<a id="xref-ref-10-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10">10–14</a>). Some studies reported higher estrogen levels among migraineurs (<a id="xref-ref-10-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10">10–12</a>), while other studies did not observe a difference (<a id="xref-ref-13-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-13">13</a>). Further large-scale studies are warranted to unveil potential associations between migraine and breast cancer.
               </p>
               <p id="p-7" class="indent">Therefore, our aims in this study are three-fold: 1) to evaluate the association between migraine and incident breast cancer
                  in a large prospective cohort, 2) to assess whether migraineurs have different circulating endogenous sex hormone levels,
                  and 3) to perform a meta-analysis of the current evidence on migraine and invasive breast cancer risk.
               </p>

                  <h2 class="">Methods</h2>
                  <div id="sec-6" class="subsection">
                     <h3>Nurses’ Health Study II</h3>
                     <div id="sec-7" class="subsection">
                        <h4>Study Population</h4>
                        <p id="p-8">Established in 1989, the Nurses<strong>’</strong> Health Study II (NHS II) is an ongoing cohort study of 116 430 US nurses who were age 25 to 42 years at baseline. Every two
                           years, information on reproductive variables, lifestyle factors, and disease outcomes was collected via questionnaires. Follow-up
                           rates at each questionnaire cycle consistently have been 85% to 90%.
                        </p>
                     </div>
                     <div id="sec-8" class="subsection">
                        <h4>Assessment of Migraine</h4>
                        <p id="p-9">Women first reported whether they had been diagnosed with migraine by a physician on the 1989 baseline questionnaire; this
                           information was updated in 1993 and 1995. Once a woman reported a diagnosis of migraine she was considered a migraineur for
                           all of the following follow-up cycles. In another study of female health professionals, agreement between self-reported migraine
                           and 2004 diagnostic criteria of the International Headache Society was high (87%) (<a id="xref-ref-15-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15">15</a>).
                        </p>
                     </div>
                     <div id="sec-9" class="subsection">
                        <h4>Assessment of Breast Cancer</h4>
                        <p id="p-10">Breast cancer case patients were identified on biennial questionnaires or through death records. Participants (or next of
                           kin) were asked for permission to collect relevant medical records. Study investigators, blinded to migraine status, reviewed
                           the records and abstracted data on tumor characteristics. A diagnosis of breast cancer confirmed by the participant, but not
                           confirmed by medical records, was included as a case in this analysis, as we confirm 99% of reported case patients for whom
                           we are able to obtain medical records. All diagnoses included in the analyses were confirmed by the participant; we were able
                           to obtain medical records and confirm 90% of these cases. Data abstracted from pathology reports were used to distinguish
                           between in situ and invasive malignancies and to classify invasive tumors by joint estrogen receptor (ER) and progesterone
                           receptor (PR) status (ER+/PR+, ER+/PR-, and ER-/PR-) as well as histologic subtypes (ductal, lobular, both ductal, and lobular).
                           Our primary outcome was defined as invasive or in situ breast cancer.
                        </p>
                     </div>
                     <div id="sec-10" class="subsection">
                        <h4>Ascertainment of Endogenous Serum Hormone Levels</h4>
                        <p id="p-11">From 1996<strong>–</strong>1999, 29 611 NHSII members age 32 to 45 years provided a blood sample. The methods of this substudy have been previously described
                           (<a id="xref-ref-16-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-16">16</a>). Briefly, 18 521 premenopausal women who were not currently pregnant, breastfed in the last six months, or on oral contraceptives
                           (OCs) were asked to provide a follicular sample (days 3<strong>–</strong>5 of their menstrual cycle) and a luteal sample (7<strong>–</strong>9 days before the anticipated start of the next menstrual period). Eleven thousand ninety women provided untimed blood samples.
                        </p>
                        <p id="p-12" class="indent">As previously described in detail (<a id="xref-ref-17-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-17">17</a>), we included 2052 premenopausal control patients from several prior nested case-control studies in the NHSII, including
                           control patients from studies of breast cancer (<em>n</em> = 1268), ovarian cancer (<em>n</em> = 46), endometriosis (<em>n</em> = 592), and rheumatoid arthritis (<em>n</em> = 19), or participants in hormone reproducibility studies (<em>n</em> = 109) with previously measured hormone levels in our cross-sectional study.
                        </p>
                        <p id="p-13" class="indent">Hormone assay methods for estrogens, androgens, progesterone, and prolactin have been described in detail elsewhere (<a id="xref-ref-17-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-17">17</a>,<a id="xref-ref-18-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-18">18</a>). Briefly, plasma levels were assayed in up to nine batches and were measured in: 1) luteal and follicular samples (estrone,
                           estradiol, estrone sulfate), 2) luteal and/or follicular as well as untimed samples (prolactin), 3) luteal samples only (progesterone),
                           and 4) luteal and untimed samples (testosterone, androstenedione, dehydroepiandrosterone [DHEA], DHEA-sulfate [DHEAS], sex-hormone
                           binding globulin [SHBG]). In prolactin analyses, we used the average prolactin level of the follicular and luteal sample in
                           women who provided blood samples at both time points, as previously published (<a id="xref-ref-19-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-19">19</a>). The methods of Sodergard were used to calculate free estradiol and free testosterone levels (<a id="xref-ref-20-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>).
                        </p>
                        <p id="p-14" class="indent">The study was approved by the Committee on the Use of Human Subjects in Research at the Brigham and Women’s Hospital. Informed
                           consent was implied by receipt of completed questionnaires and blood samples.
                        </p>
                     </div>
                  </div>
                  <div id="sec-11" class="subsection">
                     <h3>Statistical Analysis</h3>
                     <div id="sec-12" class="subsection">
                        <h4>Breast Cancer Risk</h4>
                        <p id="p-15">For the analysis of breast cancer risk, women with a cancer diagnosis (except nonmelanoma skin cancer) prior to 1989 were
                           excluded (<em>n</em> = 1052), leaving a total of 115 378 women. Participants accrued person-time from the time of the return of the 1989 questionnaire
                           until the date of breast cancer diagnosis, diagnosis of other cancer (except nonmelanoma skin cancer), death, or the end of
                           follow-up (June 1, 2009), whichever occurred first. We used Cox proportional hazards regression, with age in months and two-year
                           questionnaire cycle as the time scale, to estimate the hazard ratios (HRs) and 95% confidence intervals (CIs). The initial
                           model adjusted for age and time period, and the multivariable model adjusted for the following factors: body mass index (BMI)
                           (continuous), family history of breast cancer (yes, no), parity (nulliparous, 1, 2, ≥3 children), age at first birth (&lt;25,
                           25–29, ≥30 years), breastfeeding (0–6, 6–18, ≥18 months, missing), OC use (never, &lt;1, 1–5, ≥5 years), age at menarche (&lt;12,
                           12, 13, ≥14 years), smoking status (never, past, current), alcohol intake (none, 0–5, ≥5g/day), nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory
                           drug (NSAID) use (never, past, current), menopausal status (premenopausal, postmenopausal, dubious), and postmenopausal hormone
                           (PMH) use (estrogen only, estrogen and progesterone, other). To assess whether results varied by menopausal status (premenopausal,
                           postmenopausal), BMI (&lt;25, ≥25kg/m<sup><strong>2</strong></sup>), NSAID use (never, past, current), PMH use (never, ever) and OC use (never, ever), we compared models with and without the
                           interaction terms using the likelihood ratio test. As a sensitivity analysis, we classified women according to migraine status
                           at baseline only. We also restricted to breast cancer cases that were confirmed upon medial record review in sensitivity analyses.
                           The proportional hazards assumption was tested by comparing a model with interaction terms between migraine and age (dichotomized
                           at age 45 years) to a model without interaction terms using the likelihood ratio test. We did not observe a statistically
                           significant violation of proportional hazards.
                        </p>
                     </div>
                     <div id="sec-13" class="subsection">
                        <h4>Premenopausal Hormone Levels</h4>
                        <p id="p-16">After exclusion of up to 13 outlying values (range: 0 [estrone sulfate, DHEA, DHEAS] to 13 [prolactin]) determined using the
                           generalized extreme studentized deviate many-outlier detection method (<a id="xref-ref-21-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-21">21</a>), 2034 women who were control patients in prior nested matched case-control studies were included in our analyses. All hormone
                           levels were adjusted for batch according to the methods described by Rosner et al. (<a id="xref-ref-22-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-22">22</a>). Hormone levels were log-transformed, and generalized linear models were used to determine the percent difference in the
                           adjusted geometric means comparing migraineurs to non-migraineurs. All models were adjusted for matching factors including
                           age at (continuous)/time of day of blood draw (1–8 a.m., 9 a.m. to noon, 1–4 p.m., 5 p.m. to 12 a.m.), fasting status (&lt;10,
                           ≥10 hours; follicular and luteal phase), and race/ethnicity (Caucasian, other). In addition, all models were adjusted for
                           potential confounders including: BMI (continuous), duration of OC use (never, &lt;1, 1–5, ≥5 years), parity (nulliparous, 1,
                           2, ≥3 children), age at first birth (&lt;25, 25–29, ≥30 years), smoking history (never, past, current), alcohol intake (none,
                           0–5, ≥5g/day), and regular use of NSAIDs (yes, no). Models for luteal, random, and average of timed samples were also adjusted
                           for date of blood draw (continuous), as well as the difference between luteal blood draw date and date of next menstrual period
                           (3–7, 8–21 days, unknown/untimed). We conducted a priori sensitivity analyses restricted to ovulatory cycles for luteal estrogens
                           (defined as mid-luteal progesterone ≥400ng/dL).
                        </p>
                        <p id="p-17" class="indent">All NHS II analyses were performed with SAS version 9.2 (SAS, Cary, NC). All <em>P</em> values were two-sided and a <em>P</em> value under .05 was considered statistically significant.
                        </p>
                     </div>
                  </div>
                  <div id="sec-14" class="subsection">
                     <h3>Meta-Analysis</h3>
                     <p id="p-18">We used the Meta-Analysis of Observational Studies in Epidemiology (MOOSE) guidelines for this meta-analysis (<a id="xref-ref-23-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-23">23</a>).
                     </p>
                     <div id="sec-15" class="subsection">
                        <h4>Data Sources and Search Strategy</h4>
                        <p id="p-19">Two investigators (ACW and MSR) independently conducted literature searches in the MEDLINE, EMBASE, and Science Citation Index
                           databases from the earliest available online year of indexing up through October 2013 without language restriction using the
                           keyword “migraine” combined with the terms “breast neoplasms,” “breast,” “cancer,” or “breast cancer.”
                        </p>
                     </div>
                     <div id="sec-16" class="subsection">
                        <h4>Study Selection and Data Extraction</h4>
                        <p id="p-20">We defined a priori the following study inclusion criteria. Only studies that provided an effect estimate and standard error
                           (SE) or sufficient data to calculate these factors were included. One investigator (MSR) abstracted effect estimates and <em>P</em> values or 95% confidence intervals from the articles and entered the information in a database. The abstracted data was verified
                           by a second investigator (ACW).
                        </p>
                     </div>
                     <div id="sec-17" class="subsection">
                        <h4>Statistical Analyses</h4>
                        <p id="p-21">We performed a meta-analysis of the association between migraine and invasive breast cancer. Furthermore, we ran stratified
                           analyses according to study design, joint hormone receptor status, and histologic subtype. We used estimates adjusted for
                           multiple confounders when available and made the assumption that the odds ratios (ORs) from case-control studies approximated
                           hazard ratios. Standard errors were calculated from the 95% confidence intervals or <em>P</em> values. We decided a priori to run random effect models to calculate the summary relative risk (RR) estimates and 95% confidence
                           intervals. To evaluate heterogeneity between studies, we performed the DerSimonian and Laird Q test and additionally calculated
                           the I<sup><strong>2</strong></sup> statistic. Publication bias was assessed using Begg’s and Egger’s tests. We performed meta-regression analyses to evaluate
                           whether effect estimates differed according to study design (case-control vs cohort study), hormone receptor status (ER+/PR-
                           or ER-/PR- vs ER+/PR+), and histologic subtype (lobular vs ductal).
                        </p>
                        <p id="p-22" class="indent">All analyses for the meta-analysis were performed using Stata/SE version 12 for Windows. All <em>P</em> values were two-tailed and a <em>P</em> value under .05 was considered statistically significant.
                        </p>
                     </div>
                  </div>

                     <h3>Meta-Analysis</h3>
                     <p id="p-36">Each investigator independently identified a total of 585 records (MEDLINE: 64 records, EMBASE: 456 records, Science Citation
                        Index: 65 records), 82 of which were duplicate records for a total of 503 unique records. After title and abstract review,
                        494 records were excluded and nine articles retrieved for more detailed review. Five of these records (four full articles
                        [3–6] and one conference abstract [24]) met our inclusion criteria (<a href="http://jnci.oxfordjournals.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1093/jnci/dju381/-/DC1">Supplementary Figure 1</a>, available online). In addition to the four full articles retrieved from the database search, we included the results of
                        the present study in the primary meta-analysis. The conference abstract was excluded from our primary analyses but was included
                        in a sensitivity analysis.
                     </p>
                     <p id="p-37" class="indent">
                        <a href="http://jnci.oxfordjournals.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1093/jnci/dju381/-/DC1">Supplementary Table 2</a> (available online) summarizes the characteristics of the six studies. One case-control study did not report an overall adjusted
                        effect estimate for invasive breast cancer, and we therefore included the ORs for ductal and lobular invasive tumors in our
                        main pooled analysis (<a id="xref-ref-5-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-5">5</a>).
                     </p>
                     <p id="p-38" class="indent">Overall, migraine was associated with a statistically significantly lower risk of invasive breast cancer in the five studies
                        combined (pooled RR = 0.84, 95% CI = 0.73 to 0.98) (<a id="xref-fig-1-1" class="xref-fig" href="#F1">Figure 1</a>). The association was similar for ductal and lobular breast cancer (<em>P</em>
                        <sub>difference</sub> = .41). While there was a statistically significant inverse association with ER+/PR+ tumors (pooled RR = 0.80, 95% CI = 0.68
                        to 0.94), there was no association with ER-/PR- tumors (<em>P</em>
                        <sub>difference</sub> = .12). When we stratified our analysis by study design, there was a statistically significant inverse association in the
                        two pooled case-control studies (pooled RR = 0.72, 95% CI = 0.66 to 0.79), but no association between migraine and invasive
                        breast cancer in the three pooled prospective cohort studies (pooled RR = 0.98, 95% CI = 0.87 to 1.10, <em>P</em>
                        <sub>difference</sub> = .02) (<a id="xref-table-wrap-4-1" class="xref-table" href="#T4">Table 4</a>). There was substantial heterogeneity between studies (I<sup>2</sup> = 87%) that persisted when we restricted to prospective cohort studies (I = 72%). Overall, we found no evidence for publication
                        bias based on the Begg’s (<em>P</em> = .71) or Egger’s test statistic (<em>P</em> = .28), and the overall pooled effect estimate for the association between migraine and invasive breast cancer was not substantially
                        altered after including the conference abstract information (pooled RR = 0.83, 95% CI = 0.73 to 0.95).
                     </p>

                  <h2 class="">Discussion</h2>
                  <p id="p-42">In this large prospective cohort study of over 115 000 female nurses, self-reported physician’s diagnosis of migraine was
                     not associated with breast cancer risk. Premenopausal endogenous plasma sex hormone levels did not vary according to migraine
                     status. In the meta-analysis that included data from four previous studies as well as the current study, the pooled effect
                     estimate suggested a 16% lower risk of invasive breast cancer for migraineurs with evidence of high between-study heterogeneity.
                     When stratifying by study design, the association between migraine and invasive breast cancer risk was only apparent in case-control
                     studies.
                  </p>
                  <p id="p-43" class="indent">Our finding of a lack of a statistically significant inverse association between migraine and incident breast cancer is consistent
                     with results of a previously published prospective cohort study. Among 39 696 pre- and postmenopausal women participating
                     in the Women’s Health Study (WHS), there was no statistically significant association between breast cancer and any migraine
                     (HR = 1.10, 95% CI = 0.99 to 1.22) or for active migraine with or without aura (HR = 1.07, 95% CI = 0.89 to 1.29 and HR =
                     1.14, 95% CI = 0.98 to 1.33, respectively) (<a id="xref-ref-6-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>). Similar to our findings, there were no relationships between migraine and breast cancer risk according to hormone receptor
                     status or histology subtype.
                  </p>
                  <p id="p-44" class="indent">Among 91 116 postmenopausal women participating in the Women’s Health Initiative Observational Study (WHI-OS), migraine was
                     associated with a statistically significant 11% lower risk of breast cancer (HR = 0.89, 95% CI = 0.80–0.98) which was more
                     pronounced, though not statistically different, among older women (<a id="xref-ref-3-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-3">3</a>). In addition, the association with invasive breast cancer differed according to hormone receptor status with the lowest
                     HR for ER+/PR+ malignancies (HR = 0.83, 95% CI = 0.71–0.97). Similar to our study, migraine status was defined as self-reported
                     physician’s diagnosis. Participants in the WHI-OS were older at baseline compared with the NHSII, however migraine and breast
                     cancer risk were not associated when we stratified our analyses according to age or menopausal status. These findings are
                     in line with results from the WHS, which did not observe statistically significant differences in the association by menopausal
                     status or age (<a id="xref-ref-6-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>).
                  </p>
                  <p id="p-45" class="indent">In the two previously published case-control studies (<a id="xref-ref-4-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-4">4</a>,<a id="xref-ref-5-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-5">5</a>), migraine was associated with 26% and 33% lower risk of invasive breast cancer. One potential reason for the striking difference
                     in results compared with our findings may be study design. In both case-control studies, case patients were identified through
                     cancer registries and control patients were selected using random digit dialing. A standardized interview was performed, and
                     women were asked about exposures including a physician’s diagnosis of migraine occurring before the date of their breast cancer
                     diagnosis or reference date. Breast cancer case patients may have underreported migraine history relative to control patients,
                     possibly because of the effect of treatment on migraine symptoms (<a id="xref-ref-25-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-25">25</a>), or controls may have over-reported migraine history. However, this is speculative and there may be other factors, such
                     as unmeasured confounding, selection bias, or chance, contributing to the difference by study design.
                  </p>
                  <p id="p-46" class="indent">In the meta-analysis of four previous studies and the NHSII results, we found an overall statistically significant modest
                     inverse association between migraine and invasive breast cancer. However, this result should be interpreted carefully, as
                     there is substantial heterogeneity across studies. Study design could be identified as one possible source of heterogeneity.
                     In addition, there is some suggestion for subtle heterogeneity by hormone-receptor status. However, there was also statistically
                     significant heterogeneity within cohort studies and there may be other potential sources of heterogeneity we were unable to
                     identify and test for because of the overall small number of studies.
                  </p>
                  <p id="p-47" class="indent">Several small clinic-based studies (n = 22–142) among premenopausal women evaluated the association between various endogenous
                     circulating hormone levels and migraine status (<a id="xref-ref-10-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10">10–14</a>). Among the various endogenous hormones, estrogen and progesterone have been studied most frequently. Some studies reported
                     higher mean estrogen levels among migraineurs (<a id="xref-ref-10-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10">10–12</a>), while another study supported our finding of no difference in estrogen levels according to migraine status (<a id="xref-ref-13-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-13">13</a>). The previous evidence on progesterone is inconsistent across studies with studies showing no difference (<a id="xref-ref-12-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-12">12</a>,<a id="xref-ref-13-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-13">13</a>) or either higher (<a id="xref-ref-10-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10">10</a>) or lower progesterone levels among migraineurs (<a id="xref-ref-11-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-11">11</a>). Factors that may have contributed to the inconsistency across studies include variation in assays, study populations, migraine
                     subtypes, and sampling times and procedures. Similar to our findings, previous studies do not report statistically significant
                     differences in prolactin (<a id="xref-ref-10-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10">10</a>,<a id="xref-ref-11-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-11">11</a>), testosterone (<a id="xref-ref-14-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-14">14</a>), or DHEA-S (<a id="xref-ref-14-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-14">14</a>) levels by migraine status.
                  </p>
                  <p id="p-48" class="indent">This study has several strengths, including the large sample size, confirmation of breast cancer by medical record review,
                     and prospectively collected data on migraine history, as well as known and suspected breast cancer risk factors. Furthermore,
                     the collection of endogenous sex hormone levels was carefully timed according to the follicular and luteal phases of the menstrual
                     cycle, which allowed us to perform phase-specific analyses in a large sample of premenopausal women.
                  </p>
                  <p id="p-49" class="indent">The following limitations should be considered when interpreting our results. First, migraine information was self-reported
                     and misclassification is possible. However, the prevalence of migraine in the NHSII is similar to the prevalence observed
                     in other population-based studies (<a id="xref-ref-26-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-26">26</a>). In addition, migraine history was collected prior to breast cancer diagnosis; therefore, any misclassification should be
                     nondifferential. Second, measures of follicular and luteal endogenous hormone levels were based on blood samples collected
                     at one point in time and may not be representative of long-term average hormone levels. However, in our reproducibility study,
                     levels of most hormones at specific points in the menstrual cycle were representative over a three-year period, with the exception
                     of progesterone (<a id="xref-ref-27-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-27">27</a>). Third, the NHSII consists of predominately non-Hispanic white nurses; that may limit the generalizability of our results
                     to other populations.
                  </p>
                  <p id="p-50" class="indent">In summary, results from this large prospective cohort study do not support an inverse association between migraine and incident
                     breast cancer. In addition, women with migraine do not have different premenopausal endogenous sex hormone levels compared
                     with women without migraine. While data from two case-control studies suggest an inverse association between migraine and
                     breast cancer risk, data from recent large prospective cohort studies do not support an association in pooled analyses.
                  </p>
