                  <h2>Abstract</h2>
                  <div id="sec-1" class="subsection">
                     <p id="p-1"><strong>Background</strong> Although genetic studies have reported a number of loci associated with cutaneous melanoma (CM) risk, a comprehensive synopsis
                        of genetic association studies published in the field and systematic meta-analysis for all eligible polymorphisms have not
                        been reported.
                     </p>
                  </div>
                  <div id="sec-2" class="subsection">
                     <p id="p-2"><strong>Methods</strong> We systematically annotated data from all genetic association studies published in the CM field (n = 145), including data
                        from genome-wide association studies (GWAS), and performed random-effects meta-analyses across all eligible polymorphisms
                        on the basis of four or more independent case–control datasets in the main analyses. Supplementary analyses of three available
                        datasets derived from GWAS and GWAS-replication studies were also done. Nominally statistically significant associations between
                        polymorphisms and CM were graded for the strength of epidemiological evidence on the basis of the Human Genome Epidemiology
                        Network Venice criteria. All statistical tests were two-sided.
                     </p>
                  </div>
                  <div id="sec-3" class="subsection">
                     <p id="p-3"><strong>Results</strong> Forty-two polymorphisms across 18 independent loci evaluated in four or more datasets including candidate gene studies and
                        available GWAS data were subjected to meta-analysis. Eight loci were identified in the main meta-analyses as being associated
                        with a risk of CM (<em>P</em> &lt; .05) of which four loci showed a genome-wide statistically significant association (<em>P</em> &lt; 1 × 10<sup>−7</sup>), including 16q24.3 (<em>MC1R</em>), 20q11.22 (<em>MYH7B</em>/<em>PIGU</em>/<em>ASIP</em>), 11q14.3 (<em>TYR</em>), and 5p13.2 (<em>SLC45A2</em>). Grading of the cumulative evidence by the Venice criteria suggested strong epidemiological credibility for all four loci
                        with genome-wide statistical significance and one additional gene at 9p23 (<em>TYRP1</em>). In the supplementary meta-analyses, a locus at 9p21.3 (<em>CDKN2A</em>/<em>MTAP</em>) reached genome-wide statistical significance with CM and had strong epidemiological credibility.
                     </p>
                  </div>
                  <div id="sec-4" class="subsection">
                     <p id="p-4"><strong>Conclusions</strong> To the best of our knowledge, this is the first comprehensive field synopsis and systematic meta-analysis to identify genes
                        associated with an increased susceptibility to CM.
                     </p>
                  </div>
               </div>
               <p id="p-5">
                  <div class="boxed-text" id="boxed-text-1">
                     <div id="sec-5" class="subsection">
                        <h4>CONTEXT AND CAVEATS</h4>
                        <div id="sec-6" class="subsection">
                           <h5>Prior knowledge</h5>
                           <p id="p-6">Previous studies, including genome-wide association studies, have identified genetic variants associated with cutaneous melanoma
                              (CM).
                           </p>
                        </div>
                        <div id="sec-7" class="subsection">
                           <h5>Study design</h5>
                           <p id="p-7">Data were collected from all genetic association studies in the CM field. Meta-analyses were performed for all polymorphisms
                              identified in four or more independent case–control datasets. Supplementary meta-analyses were done for polymorphisms for
                              which three datasets from GWAS were available. The epidemiological credibility of statistically significant associations between
                              loci and CM was measured using the Human Genome Epidemiology Network Venice criteria.
                           </p>
                        </div>
                        <div id="sec-8" class="subsection">
                           <h5>Contribution</h5>
                           <p id="p-8">This is the first field synopsis and meta-analysis of genetic variants associated with CM. The main meta-analyses identified
                              eight loci statistically significantly associated with CM, five of which had genome-wide statistical significance and strong
                              epidemiological credibility. Supplementary analyses identified one additional loci with genome-wide statistical significance
                              and strong epidemiological credibility for an association with CM. The accumulated evidence was reported on the publicly accessible
                              regularly updated MelGene website (<a href="http://www.melgene.org">www.MelGene.org</a>).
                           </p>
                        </div>
                        <div id="sec-9" class="subsection">
                           <h5>Implications</h5>
                           <p id="p-9">This study comprehensively reports on the genome-wide statistical significance and epidemiogical credibility of CM susceptibility
                              genes. MelGene will provide a potential forum to report genes associated with CM as they are identified in future genetic
                              studies.
                           </p>
                        </div>
                        <div id="sec-10" class="subsection">
                           <h5>Limitations</h5>
                           <p id="p-10">Some studies may have been excluded from the analyses and random errors may be found in some of the study entries. Because
                              the meta-analyses were based on summary-level data, some potential confounders and other types of bias could not be accounted
                              for, and both gene–gene and gene–environment interactions could not be assessed. Also, as is standard of systematic meta-analyses,
                              publication bias could not be ruled out.
                           </p>
                           <p id="p-11"><em>From the Editors</em></p>
                        </div>
                     </div>
                  </div>
               </p>
               <p id="p-12">The incidence of cutaneous melanoma (CM) has steadily increased among populations of European descent during the past five
                  decades. Metastatic melanoma is the leading cause of skin cancer–related mortality and is characterized by a poor prognosis
                  and limited treatment options (<a id="xref-ref-1-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-1">1</a>,<a id="xref-ref-2-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-2">2</a>). As early detection of CM provides the best opportunity for cure, identification of high-risk individuals is an important
                  preventative strategy for reducing mortality. Susceptibility to CM is likely determined by an interaction of environmental
                  risk factors including excessive exposure to ultraviolet radiation (<a id="xref-ref-3-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-3">3</a>) and genetically controlled phenotypic traits such as nevus propensity, red or blonde hair, light-colored eyes, fair skin,
                  and limited tanning ability (<a id="xref-ref-4-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-4">4</a>,<a id="xref-ref-5-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-5">5</a>). The heritability, that is, the contribution of genetic factors to CM risk, has been estimated to be 18%–55% (<a id="xref-ref-6-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>,<a id="xref-ref-7-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-7">7</a>). Approximately, 5%–10% of patients exhibit an autosomal-dominant hereditary form of the disease, which can be caused by
                  rare highly penetrant gene mutations, such as in the cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitor 2A gene (<em>CDKN2A</em>) on chromosome 9p21, the cyclin-dependent kinase 4 gene (<em>CDK4</em>) on chromosome 12q14, and other potentially novel loci (<a id="xref-ref-8-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-8">8</a>,<a id="xref-ref-9-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9">9</a>). However, for the majority of CM patients, the genetic architecture is more complex, involving numerous genetic risk factors
                  of relatively high frequencies but low penetrance (<a id="xref-ref-10-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10">10</a>,<a id="xref-ref-11-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-11">11</a>). Among the low-penetrance genes, the most consistent association with CM has been reported for the gene encoding melanocortin-1
                  receptor (<em>MC1R</em>) on chromosome 16q24.3 (<a id="xref-ref-12-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-12">12</a>,<a id="xref-ref-13-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-13">13</a>). Other putative risk alleles involved in various cellular pathways such as pigmentation, DNA repair, oxidation stress, apoptosis,
                  cell growth, and melanocyte differentiation and migration have also been implicated in melanoma susceptibility (<a id="xref-ref-14-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-14">14</a>).
               </p>
               <p id="p-13">Within the last 15 years, approximately 150 genetic association studies including two GWAS (<a id="xref-ref-15-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15">15</a>,<a id="xref-ref-16-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-16">16</a>) have been published that claim or refute associations between CM risk and putative melanoma genes. Although highly valuable
                  for the understanding of the underlying pathogenesis and for the assessment of risk prediction, the increasing amount of information
                  has become difficult to evaluate and interpret. To assess the evidence and strength of genetic associations with CM, we have
                  collected and comprehensively cataloged all genetic association studies published in the field and conducted systematic meta-analyses
                  for all eligible polymorphisms. Systematic field synopses and meta-analyses have previously been performed for other diseases
                  using similar study designs (<a id="xref-ref-17-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-17">17</a>–<a id="xref-ref-22-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-22">22</a>). Furthermore, we have graded the epidemiological validity of nominally statistically significant meta-analysis results by
                  applying the Venice criteria proposed by the Human Genome Epidemiology Network. Detailed summaries of all association studies
                  and meta-analysis results are available on a regularly updated publicly available online database, MelGene (<a id="xref-ref-23-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-23">23</a>), which currently highlights the most promising genetic loci associated with CM risk.
               </p>
               <div class="section methods" id="sec-11">
                  <div class="section-nav"><a href="#abstract-1" title="Abstract" class="prev-section-link"><span>Previous Section</span></a><a href="#sec-22" title="Results" class="next-section-link"><span>Next Section</span></a></div>
                  <h2>Methods</h2>
                  <div id="sec-12" class="subsection">
                     <h3>Collection and Management of Eligible Studies</h3>
                     <div id="sec-13" class="subsection">
                        <h4>Search Strategy.</h4>
                        <p id="p-14">We searched the PubMed database (<a id="xref-ref-24-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-24">24</a>) by using the search terms “melanoma AND associat* AND gene*” for studies published on or before March 31, 2009, after which
                           daily PubMed searches were performed (<a id="xref-fig-1-1" class="xref-fig" href="#F1">Figure 1</a>). In addition, we searched the references of included publications and the table of contents in relevant journals on genetics,
                           dermatology, and oncology. We also searched PubMed using the keywords “melanoma AND [name of every gene identified in included
                           publications].” Furthermore, we screened the Human Genome and Epidemiology Network Navigator (<a id="xref-ref-25-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-25">25</a>) for additional publications as well as the Melanoma Molecular Maps Project (<a id="xref-ref-26-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-26">26</a>), a large online database, which reports published melanoma genetic association studies.
                        </p>

                     <div id="sec-14" class="subsection">
                        <h4>Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria.</h4>
                        <p id="p-16">Studies included in MelGene had to assess the association between a polymorphism and CM. We included all relevant case–control
                           studies that have been published in a peer-reviewed journal. We did not include those without healthy control subjects (ie,
                           studies that examined genetic associations with phenotypic variables among individuals with CM alone or among different types
                           of cancer). Studies with a family-based approach were listed in the qualitative gene summary overview on MelGene but were
                           not subjected to meta-analysis. We excluded highly penetrant mutations only present in patients but not found in control subjects.
                           We included studies on polymorphisms with three or more alleles (such as the <em>HLA</em> and <em>MICA</em> genes) in the qualitative gene summaries, but considered them for meta-analysis only if genotype frequencies of one allele
                           compared with all other alleles were consistently reported. Abstracts from conference proceedings or scientific meetings were
                           excluded. The criteria to establish a diagnosis had to be a histologically proven CM, either invasive or in situ. We excluded
                           studies of patients with non-CM (including uveal melanoma) or with metastatic disease of an unknown primary cancer. Whenever
                           an article studied more than one phenotype, only CM-specific data were included. Although publication in the English language
                           was part of the criteria for this study, no articles published in a language other than English were identified.
                        </p>
                     </div>
                     <div id="sec-15" class="subsection">
                        <h4>Genotype and Allele Distributions.</h4>
                        <p id="p-17">We used the National Center for Biotechnology Information Single Nucleotide Polymorphism Database identifiers when provided
                           (rs numbers). If an rs number was not specified in the respective publications, we generally used the most common definition
                           provided in the primary publications. Genotype distributions were extracted from eligible publications for each polymorphism
                           and listed on MelGene. Whenever allele frequencies, but not genotype frequencies, were reported in the original articles,
                           we calculated the genotype frequencies on the basis of the reported allele frequencies and sample sizes, assuming that no
                           deviations from Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium occurred, unless reported otherwise. We contacted the authors of publications with
                           missing genotype data by email and if no response was received, the respective studies were labeled as no data provided on
                           MelGene, unless there was information on odds ratios (ORs) and/or corresponding 95% confidence intervals (CIs) calculated
                           on the basis of allelic contrasts. Approximately, 3% of all genotypes remained unavailable. For studies of overlapping populations,
                           we included only one study in the respective meta-analyses, and whenever possible, the study with the largest sample size
                           was included.
                        </p>
                     </div>
                     <div id="sec-16" class="subsection">
                        <h4>GWAS and GWAS-Replication Studies.</h4>
                        <p id="p-18">Because of the absence of publicly available CM-GWAS datasets, we extracted the allele frequencies or per-allele odds ratios
                           (<a id="xref-ref-15-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15">15</a>,<a id="xref-ref-16-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-16">16</a>) from the original GWAS publications and included them in the main meta-analyses when applicable. We also included data from
                           GWAS-replication studies, that is, studies assessing the association of melanoma with selected variants derived from GWAS
                           on CM-related traits including hair, eye, and skin pigmentation; basal cell carcinoma; and melanocytic nevi (<a id="xref-ref-27-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-27">27</a>–<a id="xref-ref-31-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-31">31</a>). To capture all the important information from the limited number of GWAS and GWAS-replication studies, we also performed
                           supplementary meta-analyses on polymorphisms for which only three datasets from CM-GWAS and/or GWAS-replication datasets were
                           available (<a id="xref-ref-27-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-27">27</a>–<a id="xref-ref-29-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-29">29</a>).
                        </p>
                     </div>
                  </div>
                  <div id="sec-17" class="subsection">
                     <h3>Statistical Analyses</h3>
                     <div id="sec-18" class="subsection">
                        <h4>Meta-analyses.</h4>
                        <p id="p-19">Random-effects summary odds ratios and 95% confidence intervals (<a id="xref-ref-32-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-32">32</a>) were calculated on the basis of study-specific unadjusted odds ratios and 95% confidence intervals using allelic contrasts
                           for all variants with case–control genotype data available from at least four independent datasets in the main meta-analyses,
                           and for variants with only three case–control datasets derived from CM-GWAS and GWAS-replication studies in the supplementary
                           meta-analyses. The main meta-analyses were first performed on all datasets regardless of patient ancestry. Summary odds ratios
                           and 95% confidence intervals were also calculated after stratification for different ancestries if three or more such datasets
                           existed and were applicable only to datasets of European ancestry. In addition, for this study, dominant and recessive models
                           were assessed by meta-analysis on all eligible polymorphisms following the same inclusion and exclusion criteria. Genome-wide
                           statistical significance (<em>P</em> &lt; 1 × 10<sup>−7</sup>) was determined after including all eligible datasets without further adjustment for multiple comparisons. All statistical
                           tests were two-sided. Meta-analysis results are displayed on MelGene for each eligible polymorphism as forest plots and as
                           cumulative meta-analyses summarizing the evolvement of the summary effect estimate over time.
                        </p>
                     </div>
                     <div id="sec-19" class="subsection">
                        <h4>Sensitivity Analyses and Between-Study Heterogeneity.</h4>
                        <p id="p-20">The sensitivity analyses of the main meta-analyses results entailed calculating summary odds ratios and 95% confidence intervals
                           for all studies excluding the initial report and after excluding studies violating Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium in control subjects
                           according to a χ<sup>2</sup> test implemented in the Hardy–Weinberg R package (<em>P</em> ≤ .05). When cell counts were below five, Fisher exact test was used. Between-study heterogeneity was assessed by calculating
                           the <em>I</em><sup>2</sup> heterogeneity metric. <em>I</em><sup>2</sup> is estimated by the Q statistic (<em>I</em><sup>2</sup> = [(Q − <em>df</em>)/Q] × 100) and describes the percentage of variability in point estimates that is because of heterogeneity rather than sampling
                           error (<a id="xref-ref-33-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-33">33</a>,<a id="xref-ref-34-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-34">34</a>). Contrary to the Q statistic, <em>I</em><sup>2</sup> does not depend on the number of studies and can be compared across meta-analysis results calculated from different sample
                           sizes. Generally, values greater than 50% represent high heterogeneity (<a id="xref-ref-33-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-33">33</a>,<a id="xref-ref-34-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-34">34</a>). When there are few studies, both Q and <em>I</em><sup>2</sup> carry considerable uncertainty and should be interpreted cautiously (<a id="xref-ref-35-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-35">35</a>).
                        </p>
                     </div>
                     <div id="sec-20" class="subsection">
                        <h4>Estimating the Credibility of Statistically Significant Associations.</h4>
                        <p id="p-21">Each nominally statistically significant result of the main meta-analyses was graded on the basis of the Human Genome Epidemiology
                           Network Venice criteria for the assessment of cumulative evidence of genetic associations (<a id="xref-ref-36-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-36">36</a>,<a id="xref-ref-37-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-37">37</a>). These criteria take into account the amount of evidence (sample size measured as the number of minor alleles), consistency
                           of replication (heterogeneity across studies measured as <em>I</em><sup>2</sup>), and protection from bias [the bias reason, in particular including sensitivity analysis as outlined above, assessments
                           of the strength of the association, small-study bias (<a id="xref-ref-38-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-38">38</a>), and evidence for an excess of statistically significant results (<a id="xref-ref-39-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-39">39</a>)]. On the basis of the analysis, the overall epidemiological credibility was graded as strong (grade A), moderate (grade
                           B), or weak (grade C). For more details on these criteria, see the <a href="http://jnci.oxfordjournals.org/cgi/content/full/djr219/dc1">Supplementary Methods</a> (available online).
                        </p>
                     </div>
                  </div>
                  <div id="sec-21" class="subsection">
                     <h3>The MelGene Database</h3>
                     <p id="p-22">Demographic details of the studies included in our meta-analyses, genotype data, forest plots, and cumulative meta-analyses
                        results can be found on the publicly available continuously updated MelGene website (<a href="http://www.melgene.org">http://www.melgene.org</a>) (<a id="xref-ref-23-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-23">23</a>).
                     </p>

                  <h2>Results</h2>
                  <div id="sec-23" class="subsection">
                     <h3>Literature Searches</h3>
                     <p id="p-23">After an initial screening of 3113 articles, 145 individual publications reporting on 745 genetic variants in 181 different
                        genes were included on MelGene at the time of the data freeze on July 31, 2010 (<a id="xref-fig-1-2" class="xref-fig" href="#F1">Figure 1</a>), including two CM-GWAS (<a id="xref-ref-15-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15">15</a>,<a id="xref-ref-16-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-16">16</a>). Both the number of polymorphisms evaluated across all publications and the combined sample size per publication have steadily
                        increased in the past 20 years, with a median of 12 polymorphisms (interquartile range [IQR] = 1–37) and 985 combined patients
                        and control subjects (IQR = 330–1781) between 1992 and 2002, and a median of 82 polymorphisms (IQR = 56–195) and 12613 combined
                        patients and control subjects (IQR = 10 272–20 501) between 2002 and 2010. The most extensively studied genes include <em>MC1R</em>, the vitamin D receptor (<em>VDR</em>), and the agouti signaling protein (<em>ASIP</em>) (18%, 7%, and 6% of the publications listed on MelGene, respectively) (<a href="http://jnci.oxfordjournals.org/cgi/content/full/djr219/dc1">Supplementary Figure 1</a>, available online).
                     </p>
                  </div>
                  <div id="sec-24" class="subsection">
                     <h3>Results of the Main Meta-analyses</h3>
                     <p id="p-24">Of the 745 polymorphisms included on MelGene, 42 variants in 18 loci fulfilled our criteria for meta-analysis with data available
                        from at least four independent case–control datasets. The meta-analyses were conducted on the basis of a median of six independent
                        case–control datasets (IQR = 5–9). For the number of combined patients and control subjects in specific datasets, see <a href="http://jnci.oxfordjournals.org/cgi/content/full/djr219/dc1">Supplementary Table 1</a> (available online). Of the variants analyzed, 19 (45%) of 42 in eight loci had nominally statistically significant associations
                        with CM (<em>P</em> &lt; .05) after inclusion of all ethnicities (<a id="xref-table-wrap-1-1" class="xref-table" href="#T1">Table 1</a> and <a href="http://jnci.oxfordjournals.org/cgi/content/full/djr219/dc1">Supplementary Table 1</a>, available online). These loci comprise the chromosomal region 5p15.33 containing the genes encoding telomerase reverse transcriptase
                        (<em>TERT</em>) and cleft lip and palate-associated transmembrane protein 1-like protein (<em>CLPTM1L</em>); 5p13.2 containing the gene that encodes solute carrier family 45 member 2 (<em>SLC45A2</em>); 9p23 containing the gene for tyrosinase-related protein 1 (<em>TYRP1</em>); 9p21.3 containing <em>CDKN2A</em>; 11q14.3 containing the gene encoding tyrosinase (<em>TYR</em>); 12q13.11 containing <em>VDR</em>; 16q24.3 containing <em>MC1R</em>; and 20q11.22 containing the gene <em>ASIP</em>, and genes encoding the myosin heavy chain 7B (<em>MYH7B</em>) and the phosphatidylinositol glycan anchor biosynthesis class U protein (<em>PIGU</em>). Stratification for European ancestry when applicable did not change the overall results (the ancestry-specific meta-analysis
                        results are displayed on the respective forest plots in <a href="http://jnci.oxfordjournals.org/cgi/content/full/djr219/dc1">Supplementary Figure 2</a>, available online). The MelGene website continues to be updated with newly published studies (<a id="xref-ref-23-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-23">23</a>).
                     </p>

                  <div id="sec-25" class="subsection">
                     <h3>Genetic Variants Nominally Statistically Significantly Associated With CM in the Main Meta-analyses</h3>
                     <p id="p-32">The median allelic summary odds ratio was 1.37 (range = 1.05–3.04) for all statistically significant polymorphisms (<em>P</em> &lt; .05) on the basis of a median of seven independent datasets (IQR = 4–19; <a href="http://jnci.oxfordjournals.org/cgi/content/full/djr219/dc1">Supplementary Table 1</a>, available online). To account for the impact of loci with multiple associated single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), the
                        summary odds ratio was also calculated for the best result per gene (defined according to the SNP with the best Venice score
                        and then according to <em>P</em>), yielding a median summary odds ratio of 1.25 (range = 1.12–2.50). Four loci achieved genome-wide statistical significance
                        (<em>P</em> &lt; 1 × 10<sup>−7</sup>), namely <em>MC1R</em> (rs1805007, rs1805008, and rs1805009), <em>MYH7B/PIGU</em> (rs1885120 and rs910873), <em>TYR</em> (rs1126809 and rs1393350), and <em>SLC45A2</em> (rs16891982; <a href="http://jnci.oxfordjournals.org/cgi/content/full/djr219/dc1">Supplementary Table 1</a>, available online). Dominant and recessive meta-analysis models were also calculated, but neither provided evidence for the
                        association of additional loci with CM nor did the statistical significance of the associations change substantially (data
                        not shown). Twenty-three SNPs in 11 genes did not show statistical significance for association with CM (<em>P</em> ≥ .05) upon meta-analysis, neither on combination of all ancestries nor after stratification for European ancestry (<a href="http://jnci.oxfordjournals.org/cgi/content/full/djr219/dc1">Supplementary Table 1</a>, available online). The sample size was statistically significantly different by Wilcoxon rank sum test when compared with
                        previously reported statistically significant findings (<em>P</em> &lt; .001). For the sample sizes of the individual non-statistically significant polymorphisms, see <a href="http://jnci.oxfordjournals.org/cgi/content/full/djr219/dc1">Supplementary Table 1</a> (available online). Of note, none of these non-statistically significant polymorphisms have been reported as associated with
                        CM in a GWAS (<a id="xref-ref-15-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15">15</a>,<a id="xref-ref-16-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-16">16</a>), although coverage of some loci with the current genotyping platforms may not be optimal.
                     </p>

                  <div id="sec-26" class="subsection">
                     <h3>Supplementary Meta-analyses on Data From GWAS and GWAS-Replication Studies</h3>
                     <p id="p-33">To assess the evidence for the association of prominent GWAS-identified loci with CM for which less than four independent
                        datasets were available, we performed supplementary meta-analyses on SNPs with only three available datasets (<a id="xref-table-wrap-2-1" class="xref-table" href="#T2">Table 2</a> and <a href="http://jnci.oxfordjournals.org/cgi/content/full/djr219/dc1">Supplementary Table 2</a>, available online). The results of these analyses confirmed the genome-wide statistically significant association of the
                        chromosome 16q24.3 locus (where <em>MC1R</em> lies), with additional markers identified in this region (rs258322 <em>P</em> = 4 × 10<sup>−18</sup> and rs4785763 <em>P</em> = 5 × 10<sup>−20</sup>). Furthermore, evidence for a genome-wide statistical association with CM was confirmed for the locus on chromosome 20q11.23
                        containing the genes <em>ASIP</em>, <em>MYH7B</em>, and <em>PIGU</em> (rs1204552, <em>P</em> = 7.1 × 10<sup>−9</sup>), and for the locus on chromosome 9p21.3 containing the genes <em>MTAP</em> (encoding methylthioadenosine phosphorylase) and <em>CDKN2A</em> (rs2218220, rs1335510, rs10757257, and rs7023329; <em>P</em> = 3.0 × 10<sup>−11</sup> to 5.9 × 10<sup>−9</sup>). On chromosome 20q11.23, rs1204552 is in strong linkage disequilibrium with rs1885120 of the <em>ASIP/MYH7B/PIGU</em> locus (<em>r</em><sup>2</sup> = 0.68 on the basis of HapMap CEU) that showed genome-wide statistical significance in the main meta-analyses (<a id="xref-table-wrap-1-2" class="xref-table" href="#T1">Table 1</a>), and thus likely represents the same underlying association signal. Regarding the <em>CDKN2A</em>/<em>MTAP</em> locus on chromosome 9p21.3, <em>MTAP</em> and <em>CDKN2A</em> are located in close vicinity to each other—both genes span a chromosomal interval of approximately 175 kb, but common variants
                        in <em>MTAP</em> that show genome-wide statistically significant association in the supplementary meta-analyses are in no or only very weak
                        linkage disequilibrium with common variants in <em>CDKN2A</em> [according to the HapMap CEU + TSI sample, release II + III, August 2010 (<a id="xref-ref-41-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-41">41</a>)]. Influence on the statistical significance of the association of <em>MTAP</em> with CM by rare functional variants in <em>CDKN2A</em> occurring on different genetic backgrounds cannot be excluded. In this context, it is noteworthy that the sample of Bishop
                        et al. (<a id="xref-ref-15-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15">15</a>) was enriched for familial early-onset patients (<a id="xref-ref-42-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-42">42</a>).
                     </p>

                     <h3>Grading of Associations</h3>
                     <p id="p-39"><a id="xref-table-wrap-1-3" class="xref-table" href="#T1">Table 1</a> lists the variant with the best graded result per genetic locus when applying the Venice interim criteria (<a id="xref-ref-36-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-36">36</a>,<a id="xref-ref-37-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-37">37</a>) to all meta-analysis results with at least nominal statistical significance for association with CM. For a list of all Venice-graded

                        (<em>SLC45A2, TYRP1, TYR, MC1R,</em> and <em>MYH7B</em>), whereas three loci (<em>CDKN2A, VDR,</em> and <em>CLPTM1L</em>) were found to have only weak credibility (grade C) because of criterion 3 (protection from bias). The main reasons for low

                        be dissipated even by relatively small biases in a meta-analysis of published data (eg, <em>CLPTM1L</em> and <em>VDR</em>), or loss of statistical significance after excluding the initial study and exclusion of Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium–violating

                        epidemiological credibility with a grade of A (<a id="xref-table-wrap-2-2" class="xref-table" href="#T2">Table 2</a>); thus, assuming one single underlying association signal in this region, the <em>CDKN2A/MTAP</em> locus has strong cumulative evidence for association with CM as well.

                  <div id="sec-28" class="subsection">
                     <h3>Comparison With Previously Published Meta-analyses</h3>
                     <p id="p-40">Six meta-analyses in the CM field had been published as of July 31, 2010, including analysis of a total of 35 SNPs across
                        nine different genetic loci (43–48). In comparison, 83 SNPs across 18 genetic loci were meta-analyzed for our study when combining
                        main and supplementary meta-analyses. The median overall sample size for all SNPs was approximately 8400 combined patients
                        and control subjects in the previous meta-analyses, whereas our study had a median of approximately 11 500 combined patients
                        and control subjects for the same SNPs. For a detailed comparison with previous meta-analyses, see <a href="http://jnci.oxfordjournals.org/cgi/content/full/djr219/dc1">Supplementary Table 3 (49–52)</a> and <a href="http://jnci.oxfordjournals.org/cgi/content/full/djr219/dc1">Supplementary Figure 3</a> (available online).
                     </p>

                  <div class="section-nav"><a href="#sec-22" title="Results" class="prev-section-link"><span>Previous Section</span></a><a href="#fn-group-1" title="Footnotes" class="next-section-link"><span>Next Section</span></a></div>
                  <h2>Discussion</h2>
                  <p id="p-41">We have conducted, to the best of our knowledge, the first comprehensive and systematic field synopsis of genetic association
                     studies in CM. We more than doubled the number of meta-analyses on genetic variants thus far published in the field of CM.
                     Our synopsis provides an integrated perspective of the accumulated evidence of genetic associations in CM, the results of
                     which are accessible online at the dedicated, regularly updated searchable MelGene website. We identified eight independent
                     genetic loci nominally statistically significantly associated with CM, of which six (<em>MC1R, TYR, TYRP1, SLC45A2, ASIP/PIGU/MYH7B,</em> and <em>CDKN2A/MTAP)</em> were found to have strong epidemiological credibility.
                  </p>
                  <p id="p-42">Whereas most studies included in the synopsis used a candidate gene approach, the findings of two recent GWAS have also been
                     included, to the extent of the available data (<a id="xref-ref-15-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15">15</a>,<a id="xref-ref-16-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-16">16</a>). Of the six loci with strong credibility, four (<em>MC1R</em>, <em>TYR</em>, <em>TYRP1</em>, and <em>SLC24A5</em>) were previously proposed by candidate gene studies, and two (20q11.22, <em>ASIP</em>/<em>PIGU</em>/<em>MYH7B</em> and 9p21.3, <em>MTAP</em>) were first evaluated by GWAS, although adjacent candidate genes had previously been considered (<em>ASIP</em> and <em>CDKN2A</em>, respectively). Our findings suggest a complementary role for candidate studies and GWAS for CM (<a id="xref-ref-53-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-53">53</a>). In studies of other cancer phenotypes, few candidate variants have been validated by GWAS, whereas none of the newly discovered
                     variants in the respective cancer phenotypes had previously been considered in the candidate era (<a id="xref-ref-54-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-54">54</a>). It is possible that with larger sample sizes and more comprehensive platforms, some additional previously proposed candidate
                     genes for CM may reach genome-wide statistical significance. Despite the indisputable successes of recent GWAS (<a id="xref-ref-15-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15">15</a>,<a id="xref-ref-16-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-16">16</a>) and GWAS-related studies (<a id="xref-ref-27-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-27">27</a>–<a id="xref-ref-31-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-31">31</a>), the available platforms currently do not cover all human genetic variation. Therefore, it is likely that there are some
                     CM susceptibility genes that are missed by current approaches but may be captured by more comprehensive coverage through extensive
                     imputation using the 1000 genomes resource (<a id="xref-ref-55-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-55">55</a>,<a id="xref-ref-56-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-56">56</a>) and next-generation resequencing projects. In this context, MelGene provides a dynamic resource to evaluate the constantly
                     evolving evidence for those genetic associations by use of a comprehensive and up-to-date meta-analysis approach.
                  </p>
                  <p id="p-43">The loci showing the most compelling risk effect estimates in the MelGene meta-analyses contain genes that seemingly play
                     an important role in modulating pigmentary traits of the skin, hair, and/or eyes. However, these CM association findings have
                     to be interpreted cautiously because many of these candidates were tested for association with melanoma because of their association
                     to pigmentary phenotypes. <em>MC1R</em> on chromosome 16q24.3 encodes a cell-surface G-protein-coupled receptor that binds to alpha-melanocyte-stimulating hormone,
                     leading to the production of the photoprotective brown/black eumelanin (<a id="xref-ref-57-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-57">57</a>). <em>MC1R</em> loss-of-function polymorphisms have been reported to cause a shift of melanogenesis from eumelanin to the red and yellow
                     pheomelanin, resulting in lighter skin, freckles, and red hair color (<a id="xref-ref-58-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-58">58</a>,<a id="xref-ref-59-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-59">59</a>). <em>TYR</em> on chromosome 11q14.3 encodes tyrosinase, which controls the rate-limiting step of melanin biosynthesis. Rs1126809 (R402Q),
                     a common <em>TYR</em> SNP that shows the strongest association with CM in our meta-analyses, has been reported to reduce the catalytic activity
                     of tyrosinase (<a id="xref-ref-60-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-60">60</a>) and has been associated with blue vs green eyes, and skin sensitivity to the sun (<a id="xref-ref-61-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-61">61</a>). The association signal of rs1393350, yielding the second strongest association in the MelGene analyses for this locus was
                     reported as being independently associated with CM in a recent GWAS (<a id="xref-ref-15-8" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15">15</a>). Similar to <em>TYR</em>, <em>TYRP1</em> on chromosome 9p23 encodes an enzyme of the pigmentary system required for the eumelanization of melanosomes (<a id="xref-ref-62-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-62">62</a>). Germline mutations of <em>TYRP1</em> are the cause of oculocutaneous albinism type 3, and coding variants associated with CM have been associated with eye and
                     hair color (<a id="xref-ref-61-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-61">61</a>). <em>SLC45A2</em> on chromosome 5p13.2 encodes a melanocyte differentiation antigen that is overexpressed in melanoma cell lines (<a id="xref-ref-63-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-63">63</a>). For the nonsynonymous SNP rs16891982 (F374L) that is associated with CM with genome-wide statistical significance in this
                     study, the ancestral Leu allele has been found more frequently in Southern Europe and is associated with dark skin, eye, and
                     hair color in people of European ancestry (<a id="xref-ref-64-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-64">64</a>–<a id="xref-ref-66-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-66">66</a>). Finally, the two statistically significant variants at chromosome 20q11.22 fall within the <em>PIGU</em> gene (also known as <em>CDC91L1</em>), which is involved in cell cycle control and within the <em>MYH7B</em> gene, respectively. These statistically significant variants at chromosome 20q11.22 may not be disease-modifying variants,
                     but may be positional markers that highlight an unidentified melanoma risk locus adjacent to <em>ASIP</em> (<a id="xref-ref-16-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-16">16</a>). Although the identification of associated SNPs in the vicinity of <em>CDKN2A</em> (<em>MTAP</em>, 9p21.3) (<a id="xref-ref-15-9" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15">15</a>) possibly reflect the effects of highly penetrant <em>CDKN2A</em> mutations via linkage disequilibrium not tagged by currently known common variants, the concurrence of low to moderate independent
                     risk effects in the <em>MTAP</em> locus itself may directly influence melanocytic proliferation (<a id="xref-ref-42-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-42">42</a>). This hypothesis is also supported by an association signal with nevi count in <em>MTAP</em> (<a id="xref-ref-29-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-29">29</a>), suggesting a shared susceptibility of nevi and melanoma. Thus, there may be at least two genetically distinct effects influencing
                     melanoma susceptibility, that is, those mainly influencing pigmentation and those associated with nevus development (<a id="xref-ref-67-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-67">67</a>).
                  </p>
                  <p id="p-44">Our study does have some limitations. First, despite the use of different strategies to identify eligible studies for MelGene,
                     some studies may have been erroneously excluded. Also, although manual data extraction from the included studies followed
                     an established protocol, it is possible that some study entries contain random errors. In addition, our analyses focused on
                     allelic contrasts. However, we also performed an analysis of genotypic models that yielded similar results. Another limitation
                     of our study is that lack of access to individual data precludes more refined analyses and adjustment for potential confounders
                     and other types of bias, for example, pigmentation status, age, and sex which may potentially lead to false-positive or false-negative
                     meta-analysis results, or different magnitudes of effect. Furthermore, lack of individual data does not allow the assessment
                     of potential gene–gene and gene–environment interactions. We assessed potential sources of bias by extensive sensitivity analyses
                     on the data summary level, but our study is limited because potential undetectable bias can never be ruled out, particularly
                     publication bias. Although we tested for small-study effects, publication bias cannot be entirely excluded in retrospective
                     meta-analyses. Finally, several of the nominally statistically significant associations may still be false-positive results.
                     However, this is unlikely for those associations that have strong credibility on the basis of the Venice criteria and those
                     that have reached levels of genome-wide statistical significance.
                  </p>
                  <p id="p-45">In summary, we present a systematic and comprehensive assessment of the current evidence of genetic epidemiology research
                     in CM. The putative risk factors that have emerged from the meta-analyses—accessible on MelGene—represent the most promising
                     CM susceptibility genes described to date. The integration of results from future small- and large-scale genetic association
                     studies in MelGene will further expand our knowledge of the underlying genetic mechanisms of CM.
                  </p>
