                  <h2>Abstract</h2>
                  
                  <p id="p-1"><em>Background:</em> The relationship between processed meat consumption and the risk of stomach cancer is controversial. We conducted a meta-analysis
                     to summarize available evidence from cohort and case–control studies on this issue. <em>Methods:</em> We searched Medline for studies of processed meat consumption and stomach cancer published from January 1966 through March
                     2006. Random-effects models were used to pool the relative risks from individual studies. All statistical tests were two-sided.
                     <em>Results:</em> Six prospective cohort studies (involving 2209 stomach cancer patients) and nine case–control studies (2495 case patients)
                     were eligible for inclusion in the dose–response meta-analysis of processed meat consumption. The estimated summary relative
                     risks of stomach cancer for an increase in processed meat consumption of 30 g/day, approximately half of an average serving,
                     were 1.15 (95% confidence interval [CI] = 1.04 to 1.27) for the cohort studies and 1.38 (95% CI = 1.19 to 1.60) for the case–control
                     studies. There was no statistically significant heterogeneity among the cohort studies (<em>P</em> = .42) or among the case–control studies (<em>P</em> = .19). In three cohort and four case–control studies that examined the association between bacon consumption and stomach
                     cancer, the summary relative risk was 1.37 (95% CI = 1.17 to 1.61) for the highest versus lowest intake categories of bacon,
                     without heterogeneity among these studies (<em>P</em> = .66). <em>Conclusion:</em> Increased consumption of processed meat is associated with an increased risk of stomach cancer. However, the possibility
                     that the association may be confounded or modified by other factors cannot be ruled out.
                  </p>
                  
               </div>
               <p id="p-2">Despite a steady global decline in stomach cancer incidence and mortality over the last 50 years, this malignancy remains
                  the second leading cause of cancer death worldwide <em>(<a id="xref-ref-1-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-1">1</a>)</em>. In fact, stomach cancer accounts for nearly 10% of all cancer deaths and claims approximately 700 000 lives annually <em>(<a id="xref-ref-1-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-1">1</a>)</em>. Because the prognosis is poor, identification of risk factors amenable for modification could have a marked impact on reducing
                  stomach cancer morbidity and mortality. Infection with <em>Helicobacter pylori</em> is strongly implicated in stomach cancer etiology <em>(<a id="xref-ref-2-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a id="xref-ref-3-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-3">3</a>)</em>. Nevertheless, only a small proportion of people infected with the bacterium develop stomach cancer, suggesting that such
                  infection is not sufficient itself to cause this malignancy.
               </p>
               <p id="p-3">The decline in incidence rates of stomach cancer has been proposed to be attributable, at least in part, to improvements in
                  diet and food storage and, in particular, the introduction of refrigeration. In addition to increased year-round availability
                  of fresh fruits and vegetables, refrigeration has reduced the need for salt and other methods of food preservation. Processed
                  meat includes foods preserved by salting, smoking, or adding nitrates or nitrites. Diets high in salt could damage the gastric
                  mucosa, leading to gastritis, increased DNA synthesis, and excessive cell replication <em>(<a id="xref-ref-4-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-4">4</a>–<a id="xref-ref-6-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>)</em>. Processed meat often contains, besides high amounts of salt, carcinogenic <em>N</em>-nitroso compounds <em>(<a id="xref-ref-7-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-7">7</a>–<a id="xref-ref-9-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9">9</a>)</em>. Although an association between processed meat consumption and stomach cancer risk is biologically plausible, epidemiologic
                  studies on this relationship have yielded inconsistent results.
               </p>
               <p id="p-4">A systematic and quantitative assessment of published findings on the association between processed meat consumption and risk
                  of stomach cancer is not available. Therefore, we used meta-analysis as a systematic approach to summarize evidence from cohort
                  and case–control studies on this issue.
               </p>
               <div class="section" id="sec-1">
                  <div class="section-nav"><a href="#abstract-1" title="Abstract" class="prev-section-link"><span>Previous Section</span></a><a href="#sec-6" title="RESULTS" class="next-section-link"><span>Next Section</span></a></div>
                  
                  <h2>S<span class="sc">UBJECTS AND</span> M<span class="sc">ETHODS</span></h2>
                  
                  
                  <h3>Search Strategy</h3>
                  
                  <p id="p-5">To identify epidemiologic studies of processed meat consumption and risk of stomach cancer, we conducted a literature search
                     in Medline for articles published in any language from January 1966 through March 2006. We used the following medical subject
                     heading terms and/or text words: “meat” or “foods” combined with “stomach cancer,” “stomach neoplasm,” “gastric cancer,” or
                     “gastric neoplasm.” We also searched the reference lists of pertinent publications manually to identify additional studies.
                  </p>
                  
                  
                  
                  <h3>Study Selection</h3>
                  
                  <p id="p-6">To be included in our meta-analysis, studies had to 1) be a cohort or case–control study with stomach cancer incidence or
                     mortality as the outcome and 2) provide relative risk estimates with confidence intervals (or information to compute them)
                     of stomach cancer associated with processed meat consumption. Processed meat was taken to include any of the following meat
                     items: bacon, sausage, hot dogs, salami, and ham. We considered “cured meat,” “preserved meat,” “salted meat,” and “smoked
                     meat” as equivalent to “processed meat.”
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-7">We identified 11 prospective cohort studies <em>(<a id="xref-ref-10-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10">10</a>–<a id="xref-ref-20-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>)</em> and 22 case–control studies <em>(<a id="xref-ref-21-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-21">21</a>–<a id="xref-ref-42-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-42">42</a>)</em> with data that were potentially eligible for inclusion in the meta-analysis. One cohort study <em>(<a id="xref-ref-10-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10">10</a>)</em> and three case–control studies <em>(<a id="xref-ref-27-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-27">27</a>,<a id="xref-ref-32-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-32">32</a>,<a id="xref-ref-34-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-34">34</a>)</em> were excluded for the following reasons: no relative risks <em>(<a id="xref-ref-10-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10">10</a>)</em> or confidence intervals <em>(<a id="xref-ref-27-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-27">27</a>)</em> were provided and could not be calculated, the relative risk was for 1 standard deviation difference in processed meat consumption
                     <em>(<a id="xref-ref-32-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-32">32</a>)</em>, or the exposure was nonspecific (mixed with eggs) <em>(<a id="xref-ref-34-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-34">34</a>)</em>. The remaining 10 cohort studies <em>(<a id="xref-ref-11-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-11">11</a>–<a id="xref-ref-20-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>)</em> and 19 case–control studies <em>(<a id="xref-ref-21-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-21">21</a>–<a id="xref-ref-26-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-26">26</a>,<a id="xref-ref-28-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-28">28</a>–<a id="xref-ref-31-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-31">31</a>,<a id="xref-ref-33-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-33">33</a>,<a id="xref-ref-35-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-35">35</a>–<a id="xref-ref-42-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-42">42</a>)</em> were included in the meta-analysis.
                  </p>
                  
                  
                  
                  <h3>Data Extraction</h3>
                  
                  <p id="p-8">We extracted from each publication the following data: the first author's last name, the year of publication, the country
                     in which the study was performed, study design, the sample size, the type of control subjects (in case–control studies), duration
                     of follow-up (in cohort studies), variables controlled for by matching or in the multivariable model, and the relative risks
                     with corresponding confidence intervals for each category of processed meat consumption and/or for processed meat consumption
                     as a continuous variable. From each study, we extracted the relative risk estimates that reflected the greatest degree of
                     control for potential confounders.
                  </p>
                  
                  
                  
                  <h3>Statistical Analysis</h3>
                  
                  <p id="p-9">Relative risk (RR) was used as the measure of the association of processed meat consumption with stomach cancer risk. Because
                     the absolute risk of stomach cancer is low, the odds ratios in case–control studies approximate the relative risks <em>(<a id="xref-ref-43-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-43">43</a>)</em>; we therefore report all results as relative risks for simplicity. Relative risks from individual studies and corresponding
                     standard errors (derived from the confidence intervals) were transformed to their natural logarithms to stabilize the variance
                     and to normalize the distributions. We quantified the relationship between processed meat consumption and stomach cancer risk
                     with the method of DerSimonian and Laird <em>(<a id="xref-ref-44-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-44">44</a>)</em> by using the assumptions of a random effects model, which incorporates both within- and between-study variability. When separate
                     relative risk estimates were provided for men and women <em>(<a id="xref-ref-15-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15">15</a>,<a id="xref-ref-18-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a id="xref-ref-40-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40</a>)</em>, we pooled the relative risks (weighted by the inverse of their variance) to obtain a single relative risk from each study.
                     For one case–control study <em>(<a id="xref-ref-29-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-29">29</a>)</em> that used two control groups, the relative risks based on comparison with population control subjects were used in the overall
                     meta-analysis.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-10">For the dose-response meta-analysis, we used the method proposed by Greenland and associates <em>(<a id="xref-ref-45-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-45">45</a>,<a id="xref-ref-46-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-46">46</a>)</em> to compute study-specific slopes (linear trends) from the correlated natural log of the relative risks across categories
                     of processed meat consumption. This method requires that the distribution of case patients and control subjects (or person–time)
                     and the relative risk with its variance estimate for at least three quantitative exposure categories be known. For studies
                     that did not provide the number of case patients and control subjects in each exposure category <em>(<a id="xref-ref-15-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15">15</a>,<a id="xref-ref-25-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-25">25</a>,<a id="xref-ref-29-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-29">29</a>,<a id="xref-ref-34-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-34">34</a>,<a id="xref-ref-36-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-36">36</a>,<a id="xref-ref-39-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-39">39</a>–<a id="xref-ref-41-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-41">41</a>)</em>, we estimated the slopes using variance-weighted least-squares regression models. We rescaled processed meat consumption
                     to grams per day using 50 g as the approximate average portion (serving) size <em>(<a id="xref-ref-47-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-47">47</a>)</em>. For each study, the median or mean level of consumption for each category of processed meat was assigned to each corresponding
                     relative risk. When the median or mean consumption per category was not presented in the article, we assigned the midpoint
                     of the upper and lower boundaries in each category as the average consumption. If the upper boundary of the highest category
                     was not provided, we assumed that it had the same amplitude as the preceding category. When the lowest category was open-ended,
                     the lowest boundary was assumed to be zero. For three studies that reported relative risks for processed meat consumption
                     as a continuous variable <em>(<a id="xref-ref-19-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-19">19</a>–<a id="xref-ref-21-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-21">21</a>)</em>, we used these relative risks in the dose–response analysis. We used an increase in processed meat consumption of 30 g/day,
                     which is approximately half of an average serving. This cut point was chosen a priori; the same cut point had been used in
                     a previous meta-analysis of processed meat consumption and risk of colorectal cancer <em>(<a id="xref-ref-47-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-47">47</a>)</em>. We checked for nonlinearity of the dose–response relationship between processed meat consumption and stomach cancer by estimating
                     polynomial models. However, the best-fitting model was a linear model.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-11">Statistical heterogeneity among studies was evaluated using the <em>Q</em> and <em>I<sup>2</sup></em> statistics <em>(<a id="xref-ref-48-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-48">48</a>)</em>. For the <em>Q</em> statistic, a <em>P</em>&lt;.1 was considered to be representative of statistically significant heterogeneity. <em>I</em><sup>2</sup> is the proportion of total variation contributed by between-study variation <em>(<a id="xref-ref-48-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-48">48</a>)</em>. We conducted subgroup analyses by study design (cohort versus case–control), the type of control subjects (hospital-based
                     versus population-based) in case–control studies, the location of the study (North America, Europe, or Asia), and publication
                     year (before 2000 versus after). Separate analyses were performed for cohort and case–control studies when there was statistically
                     significant heterogeneity in the summary results between these study designs. Publication bias was assessed with Egger's regression
                     asymmetry test <em>(<a id="xref-ref-49-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-49">49</a>)</em>; <em>P</em>&lt;.1 was considered to be representative of statistically significant publication bias. Statistical analyses were performed
                     with Stata, version 9.0 (StataCorp, College Station, TX). All statistical tests were two-sided.
                  </p>

                  <p id="p-12">Of the 10 prospective cohort studies, four were carried out in the United States (including one in Hawaii and one among men

In six studies, the outcome was stomach cancer incidence <em>(<a id="xref-ref-11-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-11">11</a>,<a id="xref-ref-13-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-13">13</a>,<a id="xref-ref-14-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-14">14</a>,<a id="xref-ref-17-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-17">17</a>,<a id="xref-ref-19-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-19">19</a>,<a id="xref-ref-20-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>),</em> and in four studies, the outcome was stomach cancer mortality <em>(<a id="xref-ref-12-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-12">12</a>,<a id="xref-ref-15-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15">15</a>,<a id="xref-ref-16-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-16">16</a>,<a id="xref-ref-18-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-18">18</a>)</em>.                   <h3>Processed Meat</h3>
                  
                  
                  <h4>Highest versus lowest intake category.</h4>
                  
                  <p id="p-21">Seven cohort studies <em>(<a id="xref-ref-11-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-11">11</a>,<a id="xref-ref-13-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-13">13</a>–<a id="xref-ref-16-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-16">16</a>,<a id="xref-ref-19-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-19">19</a>,<a id="xref-ref-20-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>)</em> and 14 case–control studies <em>(<a id="xref-ref-21-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-21">21</a>,<a id="xref-ref-23-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-23">23</a>–<a id="xref-ref-25-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-25">25</a>,<a id="xref-ref-29-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-29">29</a>,<a id="xref-ref-33-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-33">33</a>,<a id="xref-ref-35-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-35">35</a>–<a id="xref-ref-42-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-42">42</a>)</em> examined the relationship between processed meat consumption and stomach cancer risk. For the meta-analysis comparing the
                     highest with the lowest consumption categories, two case–control studies were excluded because processed meat consumption
                     was analyzed as a continuous variable <em>(<a id="xref-ref-21-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-21">21</a>)</em> or the study <em>(<a id="xref-ref-37-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-37">37</a>)</em> was superseded by a later publication <em>(<a id="xref-ref-42-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-42">42</a>)</em> based on the same population but with a larger sample size [the smaller study <em>(<a id="xref-ref-37-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-37">37</a>)</em> was included in the dose–response meta-analysis because processed meat consumption was not quantified in the larger study
                     <em>(<a id="xref-ref-42-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-42">42</a>)</em>].
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-22">There was statistically significant heterogeneity between the cohort and case–control studies in the summary relative risks
                     (<em>Q</em> = 2.85; <em>P</em> = .09). Combined, the seven cohort studies included 2277 stomach cancer patients. In these studies, the summary relative
                     risk of stomach cancer was 1.24 (95% confidence interval [CI] = 0.98 to 1.56; <a id="xref-fig-1-1" class="xref-fig" href="#F1">Fig. 1</a>) for individuals in the highest relative to the lowest category of processed meat consumption. The corresponding summary
                     relative risk for the 12 case–control studies (involving 3030 case patients) was 1.63 (95% CI = 1.31 to 2.01; <a id="xref-fig-2-1" class="xref-fig" href="#F2">Fig. 2</a>). Statistically significant heterogeneity was observed among the cohort studies (<em>Q</em> = 12.99; <em>P</em> = .04; <em>I</em><sup>2</sup> = 53.8%) and among the case–control studies (<em>Q</em> = 19.11; <em>P</em> = .06; <em>I</em><sup>2</sup> = 42.4%). Within each study design, the association between processed meat consumption and stomach cancer risk did not differ
                     statistically significantly by the location of the study or publication year (<em>P</em>&gt;.1 for all). No publication bias was observed (<em>P</em> = .68 for cohort studies and <em>P</em> = .93 for case–control studies).

                  <h4>Dose–response meta-analysis.</h4>
                  
                  <p id="p-25">The dose–response meta-analysis of processed meat consumption included six cohort studies (<em>N</em> = 2209 cases) <em>(<a id="xref-ref-11-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-11">11</a>,<a id="xref-ref-13-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-13">13</a>,<a id="xref-ref-15-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15">15</a>,<a id="xref-ref-16-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-16">16</a>,<a id="xref-ref-19-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-19">19</a>,<a id="xref-ref-20-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>)</em> and nine case–control studies (<em>N</em> = 2495 cases) <em>(<a id="xref-ref-21-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-21">21</a>,<a id="xref-ref-25-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-25">25</a>,<a id="xref-ref-29-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-29">29</a>,<a id="xref-ref-33-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-33">33</a>,<a id="xref-ref-35-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-35">35</a>,<a id="xref-ref-37-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-37">37</a>,<a id="xref-ref-39-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-39">39</a>–<a id="xref-ref-41-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-41">41</a>)</em>. One cohort study <em>(<a id="xref-ref-14-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-14">14</a>)</em> and five case–control studies <em>(<a id="xref-ref-23-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-23">23</a>,<a id="xref-ref-24-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-24">24</a>,<a id="xref-ref-36-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-36">36</a>,<a id="xref-ref-38-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-38">38</a>,<a id="xref-ref-42-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-42">42</a>)</em> were excluded because processed meat consumption could not be quantified <em>(<a id="xref-ref-14-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-14">14</a>,<a id="xref-ref-24-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-24">24</a>,<a id="xref-ref-38-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-38">38</a>,<a id="xref-ref-42-8" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-42">42</a>)</em> or because the range of exposure was very narrow <em>(<a id="xref-ref-23-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-23">23</a>,<a id="xref-ref-36-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-36">36</a>)</em>, leading to exaggerated regression coefficients.

                     and hospital based case–control studies (<em>Q</em> = 0.17; <em>P</em> = .68).

                  <p id="p-32">Sausage consumption was evaluated in three cohort studies <em>(<a id="xref-ref-17-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-17">17</a>,<a id="xref-ref-18-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a id="xref-ref-20-9" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>)</em> and six case–control studies <em>(<a id="xref-ref-22-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-22">22</a>,<a id="xref-ref-26-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-26">26</a>,<a id="xref-ref-28-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-28">28</a>,<a id="xref-ref-30-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-30">30</a>,<a id="xref-ref-31-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-31">31</a>,<a id="xref-ref-41-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-41">41</a>)</em>. Three case–control studies reported a statistically significant positive association between sausage consumption and stomach
                     cancer risk <em>(<a id="xref-ref-26-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-26">26</a>,<a id="xref-ref-28-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-28">28</a>,<a id="xref-ref-31-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-31">31</a>)</em>, and two case–control <em>(<a id="xref-ref-22-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-22">22</a>,<a id="xref-ref-41-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-41">41</a>)</em> and two cohort studies <em>(<a id="xref-ref-18-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-18">18</a>,<a id="xref-ref-20-10" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>)</em> found non–statistically significant increased risks (<a id="xref-fig-4-1" class="xref-fig" href="#F4">Fig. 4</a>). There was no statistically significant heterogeneity between the summary relative risks for cohort and case–control studies
                     (<em>Q</em> = 0.58; <em>P</em> = .45). Analysis of all nine studies showed a summary relative risk of 1.39 (95% CI = 1.12 to 1.73) for high versus low sausage
                     consumption; however, there was statistically significant heterogeneity among studies (<em>Q</em> = 19.52; <em>P</em> = .01; <em>I</em><sup>2</sup> = 59.0%). Publication bias was observed for all studies (<em>P</em> =.03) but not by study type (<em>P</em> = .41 for cohort studies and <em>P</em> = .11 for case–control studies).
                     
                  </p>

                  <h2>D<span class="sc">ISCUSSION</span></h2>
                  
                  <p id="p-36">Findings of this meta-analysis support a positive relationship between processed meat consumption and risk of stomach cancer.
                     Overall, an increase in processed meat consumption of 30 g/day was associated with statistically significant 15% and 38% increased
                     risks of stomach cancer in cohort studies and case–control studies, respectively. Among the individual processed meat items,
                     findings were most consistent for bacon consumption. Summary results indicate that there was a statistically significant 37%
                     higher risk of stomach cancer among those in the highest relative to the lowest category of bacon consumption.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-37">Our study has several limitations. First, as a meta-analysis of observational studies, it is prone to bias (e.g., recall and
                     selection bias) inherent in the original studies. Prospective cohort studies are less susceptible to bias than case–control
                     studies because, in the prospective design, information on exposures is collected before the diagnosis of the disease. The
                     association between processed meat consumption and stomach cancer risk was statistically significantly stronger in the case–control
                     studies than in the cohort studies. It is possible that the relationships reported from case–control studies may have been
                     overstated due to recall or interviewer bias and possible prediagnostic early symptoms in cancer patients may have led to
                     changes in dietary habits. Moreover, selection bias is of concern in the studies with low participation rates among control
                     subjects <em>(<a id="xref-ref-21-8" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-21">21</a>,<a id="xref-ref-29-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-29">29</a>,<a id="xref-ref-40-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40</a>)</em> because those who participate are likely to be more health conscious and thus might consume less meat than nonrespondents.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-38">A second limitation is that individual studies may have failed to control for potential confounders, which may introduce bias
                     in an unpredictable direction. For example, infection with <em>H. pylori</em> is an established risk factor for gastric noncardia cancer <em>(<a id="xref-ref-3-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-3">3</a>)</em>. No study controlled for <em>H. pylori</em> infection status, although one study <em>(<a id="xref-ref-19-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-19">19</a>)</em> examined whether the association between processed meat consumption and stomach cancer risk was modified by <em>H. pylori</em> infection (see below). The two prospective studies that showed a statistically significant increased risk of stomach cancer
                     associated with a high consumption of processed meat <em>(<a id="xref-ref-19-8" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-19">19</a>,<a id="xref-ref-20-13" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>)</em> both adjusted for major potential confounders, such as age, education, smoking, body size, and intakes of total energy, alcohol,
                     fruits, and vegetables. In these studies, the age-adjusted relative risks for the highest compared with the lowest category
                     of processed meat consumption [RR = 1.58, 95% CI = 1.09 to 2.29 <em>(<a id="xref-ref-19-9" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-19">19</a>)</em> and RR = 1.69, 95% CI = 1.17 to 2.45 <em>(<a id="xref-ref-20-14" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>)</em>, respectively] were similar to the multivariable relative risks [RR = 1.62, 95% CI = 1.08 to 2.41 <em>(<a id="xref-ref-19-10" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-19">19</a>)</em> and RR = 1.66, 95% CI = 1.13 to 2.45 <em>(<a id="xref-ref-20-15" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>)</em>, respectively], indicating a lack of confounding from the considered covariates. Of the case–control studies, only approximately
                     half controlled for age, sex, and education (or socioeconomic status) <em>(<a id="xref-ref-22-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-22">22</a>,<a id="xref-ref-26-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-26">26</a>,<a id="xref-ref-30-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-30">30</a>,<a id="xref-ref-35-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-35">35</a>,<a id="xref-ref-38-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-38">38</a>,<a id="xref-ref-40-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40</a>–<a id="xref-ref-42-9" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-42">42</a>)</em> or for smoking <em>(<a id="xref-ref-29-8" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-29">29</a>,<a id="xref-ref-35-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-35">35</a>–<a id="xref-ref-41-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-41">41</a>),</em> and only four adjusted for fruit and vegetable consumption <em>(<a id="xref-ref-22-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-22">22</a>,<a id="xref-ref-23-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-23">23</a>,<a id="xref-ref-25-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-25">25</a>,<a id="xref-ref-40-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40</a>)</em>. Hence, the possibility of confounding from unaccounted nondietary and dietary risk factors for stomach cancer cannot be
                     excluded.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-39">A third limitation is that our results were likely to be affected by imprecise measurement of processed meat consumption and
                     of potential confounders in the original studies. Categorization of main exposures and confounders that are measured with
                     nondifferential error (independent of disease status) may induce differential misclassification and may bias the expected
                     relative risk toward or away from the null value <em>(<a id="xref-ref-50-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-50">50</a>–<a id="xref-ref-52-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-52">52</a>)</em>. Thus, misclassification of processed meat consumption and of potential confounders in the studies included in this meta-analysis
                     might have led to an overestimate or an underestimate of the summary relative risks.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-40">A fourth limitation is that the consumption levels in the lowest and highest categories and the range of consumption varied
                     across studies. These differences may have contributed to the heterogeneity among studies in the analysis of the highest versus
                     the lowest intake categories. To account for the different ranges of processed meat consumption, we estimated for each study
                     (that provided the required data) a relative risk for an increase in intake of 30 g/day.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-41">Finally, as with any meta-analysis, possible publication bias is of concern. Only studies of sausage consumption showed statistically
                     significant publication bias. Thus, the summary results may be an overestimate of the relative risk of stomach cancer associated
                     with sausage consumption.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-42">A positive relationship between processed meat consumption and risk of stomach cancer is biologically plausible. In addition
                     to having high amounts of salt, processed meat often contains nitrite or nitrate and <em>N</em>-nitroso compounds <em>(<a id="xref-ref-7-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-7">7</a>–<a id="xref-ref-9-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9">9</a>). N</em>-Nitroso compounds are potent carcinogens that can induce tumors in various animal species at several sites <em>(<a id="xref-ref-7-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-7">7</a>,<a id="xref-ref-53-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-53">53</a>). N</em>-Nitroso compounds are also formed endogenously in the stomach through nitrosation by nitrites of amines or amides. Sugimura
                     and Fujimura <em>(<a id="xref-ref-54-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-54">54</a>)</em> showed that gastric carcinomas were induced in rats in response to oral administration of an <em>N</em>-nitroso compound, <em>N</em>-methyl-<em>N</em>′-nitro-<em>N</em>-nitrosoguanidine. Furthermore, several epidemiologic studies have reported a statistically significant positive association
                     between <em>N</em>-nitrosodimethylamine or nitrosamine intake with stomach cancer risk <em>(<a id="xref-ref-20-16" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>,<a id="xref-ref-55-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-55">55</a>–<a id="xref-ref-58-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-58">58</a>)</em>. Although salt is not intrinsically carcinogenic, it may act as an irritant to the gastric mucosa, leading to gastritis,
                     increased DNA synthesis, and cell proliferation <em>(<a id="xref-ref-4-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-4">4</a>–<a id="xref-ref-6-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>)</em>. Excess salt also makes the mucosa cells more susceptible to carcinogens from foods. In experimental studies with rats, salt
                     enhances the carcinogenic effects of gastric carcinogens, such as the <em>N</em>-nitroso compound <em>N</em>-methyl-<em>N</em>′-nitro-<em>N</em>-nitrosoguanidine <em>(<a id="xref-ref-59-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-59">59</a>–<a id="xref-ref-61-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-61">61</a>)</em>.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-43">It is possible that the relationship between processed meat consumption and stomach cancer risk is restricted to certain subgroups
                     of the population, such as those with specific genetic polymorphisms coding for enzymes involved in the metabolism of <em>N</em>-nitroso compounds or those infected with <em>H. pylori</em>. In a nested case–control study within the European Prospective Investigation Into Cancer and Nutrition <em>(<a id="xref-ref-19-11" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-19">19</a>)</em>, the positive association between processed meat consumption and stomach cancer risk appeared to be restricted to <em>H. pylori</em> antibody–positive subjects. An association with processed meat consumption may also be modified by intake of vitamins C and
                     E, fruits, and vegetables because these dietary factors can reduce the formation of <em>N</em>-nitroso compounds in the stomach <em>(<a id="xref-ref-62-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-62">62</a>)</em>. In the Swedish Mammography Cohort <em>(<a id="xref-ref-20-17" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>)</em>, the positive association of processed meat consumption with stomach cancer risk was somewhat stronger among women with a
                     low consumption of fruits and vegetables.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-44">Four studies investigated whether the association between processed meat <em>(<a id="xref-ref-19-12" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-19">19</a>,<a id="xref-ref-35-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-35">35</a>,<a id="xref-ref-39-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-39">39</a>)</em> or sausage <em>(<a id="xref-ref-26-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-26">26</a>)</em> consumption and stomach cancer risk differed by histological subtype (intestinal versus diffuse); none showed any appreciable
                     difference. In the only study that examined risk by anatomical subsite, high consumption of processed meat was associated
                     with an increased risk of gastric noncardia cancer (highest versus lowest intake category, RR = 1.92; 95% CI = 1.11 to 3.33)
                     but not of gastric cardia cancer (highest versus lowest intake category, RR = 1.14; 95% CI = 0.52 to 2.49) <em>(<a id="xref-ref-19-13" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-19">19</a>)</em>.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-45">In summary, findings of this meta-analysis support the hypothesis that high consumption of processed meat may be associated
                     with an increased risk of stomach cancer. Future studies should control for more potential confounders and examine whether
                     the association between processed meat consumption and stomach cancer risk is modified by other dietary factors, <em>H. pylori</em> infection status, or genetic polymorphisms. In addition, whether the association differs according to histologic subtype
                     or anatomic subsite warrants further study.
                  </p>
