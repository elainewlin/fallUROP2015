            <div class="article fulltext-view" itemprop="articleBody"><span class="highwire-journal-article-marker-start"></span><h1 id="article-title-1" itemprop="headline">Meta-analysis: Dietary Fat Intake, Serum Estrogen Levels, and the Risk of Breast Cancer</h1>
               <div class="contributors">

               <div class="section abstract" id="abstract-1" itemprop="description">
                  <div class="section-nav">
                     <div class="nav-placeholder"> </div><a href="#sec-1" title="M aterials and Methods" class="next-section-link"><span>Next Section</span></a></div>
                  <h2>Abstract</h2>
                  
                  <p id="p-1">BACKGROUND: There is compelling evidence that estrogens influence breast cancer risk.
                     
                     Since the mid-1980s, dietary fat intervention studies have been conducted to investigate the
                     
                     effect of fat intake on endogenous estrogen levels. To further our understanding of the possible
                     
                     relationship between dietary fat and breast cancer, we conducted a meta-analysis of dietary fat
                     
                     intervention studies that investigated serum estradiol levels, and we reviewed the nature of the
                     
                     evidence provided by prospective analytic studies of fat consumption and breast cancer risk.
                     
                     METHODS: A computerized search of the English language literature on estrogen/estradiol and
                     
                     dietary fat intervention studies published from January 1966 through June 1998 was conducted
                     
                     using the MEDLINE® database. Pooled estimates were derived from the change in estradiol
                     
                     levels associated with fat reduction from 13 studies. Analyses were conducted separately for
                     
                     premenopausal and postmenopausal women and in both groups combined. RESULTS AND
                     
                     CONCLUSIONS: Statistically significant reductions in serum estradiol levels of
                     −
                     7.4% (95% confidence interval [CI] =
                     −
                     11.7% to −2.9%) among premenopausal women and
                     −
                     23.0% (95% CI = −27.7% to −18.1%)
                     
                     among postmenopausal women were observed, with an overall −13.4%
                     
                     (95% CI = −16.6% to −10.1%) reduction observed.
                     
                     The greatest reductions occurred in two studies in which dietary fat was reduced to
                     
                     10%-12% of calories compared with 18%-25% of calories in the other
                     
                     studies. A statistically significant reduction in estradiol levels of −6.6%
                     
                     (95% CI = −10.3% to −2.7%) remained after
                     
                     exclusion of these two studies. Review of prospective analytic epidemiologic studies that
                     
                     allowed for dietary measurement error suggests that the possibility that reducing fat consumption
                     
                     below 20% of calories will reduce breast cancer risk cannot be excluded.
                     
                     IMPLICATIONS: Dietary fat reduction can result in a lowering of serum estradiol levels and
                     
                     such dietary modification may still offer an approach to breast cancer prevention.
                  </p>
                  
               </div>
               <p id="p-2">Dietary fat has been the major focus in the search for dietary causes of breast cancer, but its
                  
                  importance remains controversial (<a id="xref-ref-1-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-1">1</a>,<a id="xref-ref-2-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-2">2</a>). Although a combined analysis of
                  
                  12 case-control studies did find a statistically significant positive association between fat intake
                  
                  and risk (<a id="xref-ref-3-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-3">3</a>), it has been argued that case-control studies of this issue can
                  
                  lead to spurious associations (<a id="xref-ref-4-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-4">4</a>,<a id="xref-ref-5-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-5">5</a>). Prospective cohort studies, in which
                  
                  diet is assessed before the diagnosis of disease, are superior in this regard. In a combined analysis
                  
                  of seven cohort studies of fat intake and breast cancer risk, Hunter et al. (<a id="xref-ref-6-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>) concluded there is “no evidence of a positive association between . . . fat
                  
                  intake and risk of breast cancer” and “no reduction in risk even among women
                  
                  whose energy intake from fat was less than 20 percent of total energy intake.” However,
                  
                  in these studies, less than 2% of subjects had fat intake less than 20% and only
                  
                  6% had intake between 20% and 25% (<a id="xref-ref-6-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>). As we
                  
                  discuss below, these figures are likely to be substantial overestimates, and the assertion
                  
                  concerning the effect of an intake of less than 20% calories may not be valid (<a id="xref-ref-6-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>).
               </p>
               <p id="p-3">The lack of support of a fat-breast cancer association from prospective epidemiologic studies
                  
                  contradicts the overwhelming support for such an association from international correlational
                  
                  studies (<a id="xref-ref-2-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-2">2</a>,<a id="xref-ref-7-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-7">7</a>). Until around 1970, there existed about a sixfold difference
                  
                  in breast cancer rates between the low rates in Asia and the high rates in U.S. whites (<a id="xref-ref-8-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-8">8</a>,<a id="xref-ref-9-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9">9</a>). This large variation in risk was not due to underlying genetic differences, since
                  
                  the rates of breast cancer in Asian migrants to the United States have shifted substantially toward
                  
                  those of U.S. whites, and the rates in Japanese-Americans are now some 70% of the rates
                  
                  in U.S. whites (<a id="xref-ref-10-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10">10</a>). In recent years, women living in urban areas of Japan
                  
                  have also experienced a great increase in their breast cancer incidence (<a id="xref-ref-11-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-11">11</a>,<a id="xref-ref-12-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-12">12</a>); concurrent with this increase has been an extraordinary change in their dietary
                  
                  habits. In the early 1950s, fat comprised only some 8% of the calories in the typical
                  
                  Japanese diet, but by the late 1980s, fat consumption was some 32% of calories (<a id="xref-ref-13-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-13">13</a>,<a id="xref-ref-14-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-14">14</a>), approaching the typical U.S. level.
               </p>
               <p id="p-4">As reviewed by Welsch (<a id="xref-ref-15-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-15">15</a>), reduction in fat consumption can lower
                  
                  mammary tumor incidence in rodents. In some animal studies (<a id="xref-ref-16-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-16">16</a>-<a id="xref-ref-18-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-18">18</a>),
                  
                  the incidence of mammary tumors plateaued at about 20% of calories from fat.
               </p>
               <p id="p-5">If a threshold effect of fat can be extended to breast cancer in humans, it may help explain
                  
                  the lack of association between fat and breast cancer usually reported in studies conducted in
                  
                  Western populations, most of whom have fat intake substantially above 20% calories from
                  
                  fat. Thus, sorting out the role of fat in the lower range (i.e., &lt;20% of calories) in
                  
                  humans remains a priority.
               </p>
               <p id="p-6">There is overwhelming evidence that estrogen levels are a critical determinant of breast
                  
                  cancer risk (<a id="xref-ref-19-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-19">19</a>,<a id="xref-ref-20-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>). Women in Asia at low risk for breast cancer have
                  
                  been shown consistently to have lower urinary and blood levels of estrogens than Caucasian
                  
                  women at high risk for this disease (<a id="xref-ref-20-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>,<a id="xref-ref-21-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-21">21</a>). Strong support for a role of
                  
                  postmenopausal estrogens and risk of breast cancer was recently reported by Hankinson et al. (<a id="xref-ref-22-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-22">22</a>) using the Nurses' Health Study. In a pooled analysis of six
                  
                  prospective studies on endogenous estradiol levels and breast cancer risk, postmenopausal
                  
                  women who subsequently developed breast cancer showed a 15% higher mean
                  
                  concentration of serum estradiol than women who did not (<a id="xref-ref-23-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-23">23</a>). Similar
                  
                  differences in mean estradiol levels were seen in a pooled analysis of 16 case-control studies (<a id="xref-ref-23-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-23">23</a>).
               </p>
               <p id="p-7">Since the mid-1980s, dietary fat intervention studies have been conducted to investigate the
                  
                  effect of fat intake on endogenous estrogen levels. The assumption is that lowered estrogen levels
                  
                  could be regarded as likely to lead to lower breast cancer risk. We have conducted a quantitative
                  
                  review of published studies of the effect of dietary fat intervention studies on serum estradiol
                  
                  levels in premenopausal and postmenopausal women.
               </p>
               <div class="section" id="sec-1">

                  <h2>M <span class="sc">aterials and</span> M<span class="sc">ethods</span></h2>
                  
                  <p id="p-8"><strong>Identification of studies.</strong> We identified studies by a computerized search of the
                     
                     MEDLINE® English language literature on estrogen/estradiol and dietary fat intervention
                     
                     studies published from January 1966 through June 1998. We also reviewed the reference lists of
                     
                     the relevant publications to identify additional studies. We included only intervention studies that
                     
                     specified the level of fat consumed during the intervention period and the duration of the study
                     
                     and presented endogenous estrogen levels prior to and during (or at the completion of) the
                     
                     intervention or the percent change in endogenous estrogen level before and during intervention.
                     
                     We did not include dietary fat intervention studies that were conducted among women with
                     
                     breast cancer (<a id="xref-ref-24-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-24">24</a>-<a id="xref-ref-26-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-26">26</a>) or that investigated the effect on estrogen levels of
                     
                     different types of fats consumed (<a id="xref-ref-27-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-27">27</a>). The former group of studies was
                     
                     excluded in the present analysis because of potential confounding by adjuvant chemotherapy and
                     
                     treatment-related weight changes (<a id="xref-ref-24-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-24">24</a>-<a id="xref-ref-26-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-26">26</a>). Thus, our pooled analysis
                     
                     included 10 studies with data on premenopausal women (<a id="xref-ref-28-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-28">28</a>-<a id="xref-ref-37-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-37">37</a>) and four
                     
                     studies with data on postmenopausal women (<a id="xref-ref-35-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-35">35,38-40</a>). Although
                     
                     Crighton et al. (<a id="xref-ref-38-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-38">38</a>) presented results on premenopausal women, we
                     
                     excluded these data in our analysis because we could not determine for certain the endogenous
                     
                     estradiol levels at baseline and during the intervention period from the figures or the text of this
                     
                     paper. However, we have included results on postmenopausal women from this study (<a id="xref-ref-38-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-38">38</a>), because the change in estradiol levels, that we calculated by
                     
                     extrapolating the results presented in the figures, was compatible with the results these
                     
                     investigators described in the text.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-9"><strong>Statistical analyses.</strong> For each study, we took either the reported ratio of estradiol
                     
                     level after the low-fat diet to the level at baseline or we calculated this ratio using the estradiol
                     
                     levels reported at baseline and after dietary intervention. To calculate the standard error of this
                     
                     ratio, we used either the 95% confidence intervals (CIs) from the reports (<a id="xref-ref-31-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-31">31,34,38</a>), the reported <em>P</em> value (<a id="xref-ref-33-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-33">33</a>), or the standard
                     
                     deviation (or standard error of the mean) of the baseline and treatment estradiol levels (<a id="xref-ref-28-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-28">28-30,32,35-37,39,40</a>). The measurements (ratio and standard errors)
                     
                     were expressed in natural logarithms. Assuming that the log ratio is approximately normally
                     
                     distributed, the estimated standard errors were used to obtain a 95% CI for the log ratio
                     
                     that then was exponentiated to calculate the corresponding CI for the ratio. To summarize the
                     
                     effect of interest, summary estimates of the log ratios in estradiol levels were first described
                     
                     using fixed-effects models (<a id="xref-ref-42-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-42">42</a>). Heterogeneity was assessed by visual
                     
                     inspection of the graphic plot of data and by the heterogeneity test described by DerSimonian and
                     
                     Laird (<a id="xref-ref-43-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-43">43</a>). Analyses were conducted separately for premenopausal and
                     
                     postmenopausal women and in both groups combined. Relevant information (i.e., sample size,
                     
                     study duration, timing of blood specimen collection, dietary goals in terms of percent calories in
                     
                     fat and intake of fiber, and body weight before and after dietary change) regarding each of the
                     
                     intervention studies is described in Table 1.<a id="xref-table-wrap-1-1 class='xref-arrow-link'"><span>⇔</span></a> Study-specific estimates of
                     
                     the ratio of serum estradiol levels are shown in Fig. 1,<a id="xref-fig-1-1 class='xref-arrow-link'"><span>⇔</span></a> ordered, from top
                     
                     to bottom, by decreasing percent of fat calories during intervention by menopausal status. The
                     
                     average ratio and the corresponding CI are denoted, respectively, by a square and line through the
                     
                     square. A summary average of the ratio using all 14 studies combined is shown as a triangle,
                     
                     whereas the summary average calculated after exclusion of the two most extreme studies (<a id="xref-ref-37-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-37">37</a>,<a id="xref-ref-40-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40</a>) (<em>see below</em>) is shown as a circle. All reported <em>P</em>
                     
                     values were derived from two-sided statistical tests.
                  </p>

                  <h2>R<span class="sc">esults</span></h2>
                  
                  <p id="p-10">Table 1<a id="xref-table-wrap-1-2 class='xref-arrow-link'"><span>⇔</span></a> summarizes the dietary fat intervention studies. Subjects
                     
                     served as their own control in all studies but one (<a id="xref-ref-31-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-31">31</a>); baseline hormone
                     
                     levels were measured and were compared with measurements obtained after varying periods of
                     
                     dietary intervention. In the studies conducted among premenopausal women, subjects changed
                     
                     from a high-fat (29%-46% of fat calories) to a low-fat (12%-25% of
                     
                     fat calories) diet typically for 2 or 3 months. During the intervention period, fat intake was
                     
                     21%-25% of calories in five studies (<a id="xref-ref-28-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-28">28</a>-<a id="xref-ref-32-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-32">32</a>),
                     
                     18%-20% in four (<a id="xref-ref-33-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-33">33</a>-<a id="xref-ref-36-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-36">36</a>), and 12% in one (<a id="xref-ref-37-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-37">37</a>). As part of the intervention design, four studies (<a id="xref-ref-28-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-28">28,33,34,36</a>) showed increases of approximately 30 g of fiber per day, while in six
                     
                     studies (<a id="xref-ref-29-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-29">29-32,35,37</a>), fiber intake showed increases of 2-9 g per day.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-11">In the studies with data on postmenopausal women, the intervention period ranged from 3
                     
                     weeks (<a id="xref-ref-40-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40</a>) to 5 months (<a id="xref-ref-39-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-39">39</a>). During the
                     
                     intervention period, calories from fat was 24% in one study (<a id="xref-ref-38-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-38">38</a>),
                     
                     18%-20% in two (<a id="xref-ref-35-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-35">35</a>,<a id="xref-ref-39-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-39">39</a>), and 10% in a fourth (<a id="xref-ref-40-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40</a>). Fiber intake increased by 2 g per day in one study (<a id="xref-ref-35-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-35">35</a>), was 35-45 g per day (per 1000 kcal) as part of the intervention protocol in a second
                     
                     (<a id="xref-ref-40-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40</a>) (baseline fat and fiber intakes were not presented), and was not
                     
                     presented in two (<a id="xref-ref-38-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-38">38</a>,<a id="xref-ref-39-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-39">39</a>).
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-12">Serum estradiol levels decreased by at least 5% in seven (<a id="xref-ref-29-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-29">29-34,37</a>) of the 10 studies in premenopausal women; results reached statistical significance
                     
                     in two studies (<a id="xref-ref-38-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-38">38</a>-<a id="xref-ref-40-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40</a>)
                     
                     of the four studies showed a reduction in serum estradiol levels; results were statistically
                     
                     significant in two (<a id="xref-ref-39-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-39">39</a>,<a id="xref-ref-40-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40</a>). One study (<a id="xref-ref-35-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-35">35</a>) showed
                     
                     a small statistically nonsignificant increase.
                  </p>

                     in estradiol level of −7.4% (95% CI = −11.7% to
                     −
                     2.9%) among premenopausal women and of −23.0% (95%
                     
                     CI = −27.7% to −18.1%) among postmenopausal women; the

                     −

                     in which the fat intake was reduced to 12% or less showed the largest percent reductions.
                     
                     We repeated our pooled estimate calculations for studies in which the calories from fat was
                     
                     18%-25% by excluding these two most extreme studies (<a id="xref-ref-37-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-37">37</a>,<a id="xref-ref-40-8" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40</a>). This analysis showed a pooled estimate of a change in estradiol level of
                     −
                     6.7% (95% CI = −11.1% to −2.1%)

                  <h2>D<span class="sc">iscussion</span></h2>
                  
                  <p id="p-15">In this review of 13 dietary fat intervention studies, percent calories from fat intake changed
                     
                     to 18%-25% in 11 studies (<a id="xref-ref-28-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-28">28-36,38,39</a>), while two studies
                     
                     (<a id="xref-ref-37-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-37">37</a>,<a id="xref-ref-40-10" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40</a>) showed changes to 10% and 12% during the
                     
                     intervention period. We found in all studies combined that serum estradiol levels decreased
                     
                     statistically significantly (−13.4%) in premenopausal and postmenopausal women
                     
                     together and separately in premenopausal (−7.4%) and postmenopausal women
                     
                     (−23.0%). Statistically significant, but smaller reductions in serum estradiol levels
                     
                     were found when we restricted the analysis to the 11 studies in which fat calories changed to
                     
                     18%-25%.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-16">We have no explanation for the increase in estradiol levels found in two studies (<a id="xref-ref-35-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-35">35</a>,<a id="xref-ref-36-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-36">36</a>) that had the largest reduction in percent calories from fat intake and in which
                     
                     fat intake was 18% of calories from fat. The CIs were wide in one of these studies (<a id="xref-ref-36-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-36">36</a>) but not in the second (<a id="xref-ref-35-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-35">35</a>).
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-17">Two studies (<a id="xref-ref-37-8" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-37">37</a>,<a id="xref-ref-40-11" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40</a>) differ from the other dietary fat intervention
                     
                     studies in that the level of fat intake was much lower (10%-12% of fat calories)
                     
                     than in the other studies (18%-25% of fat calories). These diets were similar in fat
                     
                     content to that of traditional low-fat-consuming Asian women (<em>see below</em>). The largest
                     
                     reductions in estradiol levels were also found in these two studies (<a id="xref-ref-37-9" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-37">37</a>,<a id="xref-ref-40-12" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40</a>).
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-18">A concern in interpreting the effect of dietary fat reduction on blood estrogen levels is the
                     
                     possible effect of other dietary factors. Reductions in serum estradiol levels have been associated
                     
                     with increases in fiber intake while the percent calories from fat remained unchanged (<a id="xref-ref-44-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-44">44</a>,<a id="xref-ref-45-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-45">45</a>). However, the fiber-estrogen association is complex; it may
                     
                     depend on the specific type of fiber (i.e., an effect of wheat but not of oat or corn bran) (<a id="xref-ref-44-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-44">44</a>) and both the duration and amount of wheat bran supplementation (<a id="xref-ref-45-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-45">45</a>). In five of the studies reviewed here (<a id="xref-ref-28-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-28">28,33,34,36,40</a>), as part of the intervention protocol, reduction in dietary fat was accompanied by a
                     
                     large increase in all sources of fiber (i.e., grain, legume, vegetable, and fruit). Among the other
                     
                     studies (<a id="xref-ref-29-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-29">29,31,32,35,37</a>) that presented information on fiber intake, intake
                     
                     remained low (&lt;20 g per day) (<a id="xref-ref-30-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-30">30</a>,<a id="xref-ref-31-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-31">31</a>), medium (<a id="xref-ref-32-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-32">32</a>,<a id="xref-ref-37-10" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-37">37</a>), and high (<a id="xref-ref-33-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-33">33,34,40</a>) fiber intake (Table 1)<a id="xref-table-wrap-1-3 class='xref-arrow-link'"><span>⇔</span></a>. The separate effects of fiber
                     
                     and fat intake were formally evaluated in one study (<a id="xref-ref-33-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-33">33</a>); both
                     
                     macronutrients were found to have independent effects on serum estrogen levels. In this study,
                     
                     fat reduction was associated with more pronounced reductions in free estradiol and total estrone
                     
                     levels, while fiber increase appeared to produce a greater reduction in total estradiol; however,
                     
                     both had comparable reducing effects on estrone sulfate (<a id="xref-ref-33-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-33">33</a>). The effects
                     
                     of fiber intake on estrogen levels clearly warrant further investigation.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-19">Reduction in dietary fat intake was also accompanied by significant reductions in body weight in some studies (<a id="xref-ref-30-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-30">30,32,37-40</a>) but not in others (<a id="xref-ref-28-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-28">28,29,31,33-36</a>). Reductions in serum estradiol levels were found in all six studies that reported significant reductions in body weight
                     and in four of the seven studies that found no significant changes in body weight (Table 1).<a id="xref-table-wrap-1-4 class='xref-arrow-link'"><span>⇔</span></a> Investigators in two studies (<a id="xref-ref-39-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-39">39</a>,<a id="xref-ref-40-13" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40</a>) noted that the individual reductions in estradiol levels in their studies were not related to weight loss. In two other
                     studies (<a id="xref-ref-33-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-33">33</a>,<a id="xref-ref-34-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-34">34</a>) that found substantial reductions in serum estradiol levels in association with dietary fat reduction, the body weight of
                     study subjects was maintained during the intervention period. Publication bias is unlikely to explain the present findings
                     but this source of bias cannot be precluded with certainty.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-20">The effect of dietary fat on serum estradiol levels from our pooled analyses can be compared
                     
                     with the differences in serum estradiol levels reported in studies that have evaluated estradiol
                     
                     levels of Asian and white women (<a id="xref-ref-20-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-20">20</a>,<a id="xref-ref-21-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-21">21</a>). Studies conducted among
                     
                     premenopausal and postmenopausal women in Japan (<a id="xref-ref-47-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-47">47</a>,<a id="xref-ref-48-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-48">48</a>) and recent southeast Asian migrants to Hawaii (<a id="xref-ref-49-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-49">49</a>) have found that serum estradiol levels were some 30%-70% lower in
                     
                     Asians than in whites of comparable age and menopausal status. Information on intake of dietary
                     
                     fat was available in only one of these studies (<a id="xref-ref-49-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-49">49</a>). In this study,
                     
                     premenopausal Asian and Caucasian women consumed, respectively, 22% and 40%
                     
                     of calories from fat. The corresponding fat intake was 19% and 38% among
                     
                     postmenopausal women (<a id="xref-ref-49-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-49">49</a>). Thus, levels of fat intake among Caucasian
                     
                     and Asian women in this study resembled, respectively, the level of dietary fat intake at baseline
                     
                     and during intervention in the fat intervention studies we have reviewed here. The larger
                     
                     difference in estradiol levels between Asian and Caucasian women compared with the results
                     
                     obtained in the short-term dietary intervention studies may be related to the cumulative effects of
                     
                     dietary fat intake.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-21">The 7% to 13% reduction in estradiol levels in our pooled analyses is certainly
                     
                     in the range of difference (15%) that has been reported in serum estradiol levels for
                     
                     women who developed breast cancer compared with those who did not (<a id="xref-ref-23-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-23">23</a>). Prentice et al. (<a id="xref-ref-39-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-39">39</a>) calculated that a 17% reduction
                     
                     in estradiol concentration could explain the fivefold international gradient in breast cancer risk
                     
                     between populations having dietary fat intakes similar to those levels consumed before and
                     
                     during dietary intervention, as reviewed above. On this basis (<a id="xref-ref-39-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-39">39</a>),
                     
                     analytic studies on dietary fat and breast cancer risk that are truly investigating the effect of a
                     
                     20% calories from fat diet should be able to detect a reduction in breast cancer risk. It may
                     
                     be that the failure to detect this in analytic cohort studies (<a id="xref-ref-6-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>) is due to too
                     
                     few persons having a fat intake as low as 20% when allowance is made for measurement
                     
                     error in measuring fat intake. The cohort studies combined by Hunter et al. (<a id="xref-ref-6-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>) all had associated calibration substudies to enable them to be able to predict
                     ‘
                     true’ nutrient intake from the measurements made with a food-frequency
                     
                     questionnaire (FFQ). These studies take a sample of each cohort and record ‘true’
                     
                     nutrient intakes over a number of days and, on this basis, estimate separate (for each cohort)
                     
                     calibration equations of the form <em>f<sub>t</sub></em> = <em>a</em> + <em>b</em>
                     ×
                     <em>f<sub>q</sub>,</em> where <em>f<sub>t</sub></em> is the estimated true intake based
                     
                     on the intake <em>f<sub>q</sub>,</em> determined from the FFQ. The effect on the estimated
                     
                     (logistic) regression of disease incidence on nutrient intake is to change the regression coefficient
                     
                     from β to β/<em>b (<a id="xref-ref-50-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-50">50</a>,<a id="xref-ref-51-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-51">51</a>).</em> Hunter et al. (<a id="xref-ref-6-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>) reported that the effect of this on the studies they analyzed was to increase the
                     
                     breast cancer relative risk per 25 g of (calorie-adjusted) fat from 1.02 to 1.07. We can, therefore,
                     
                     estimate the average <em>b</em> from the equation 0.02/<em>b</em> = 0.07, i.e.,
                     
                     approximate average <em>b</em> = 0.28. Since calorie-adjusted fat consumption is closely
                     
                     related to percent calories from fat, we can use this <em>b</em> to draw tentative conclusions
                     
                     about the effect of measurement error on estimates of risk associated with percent calories from
                     
                     fat. A <em>b</em> of 0.28 means that an observed difference of FFQ-based percent calories from
                     
                     fat of 20%, say, is truly a difference of only (0.28 × 20%) =
                     
                     5.6%.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-22">The “20 percent” figure in the statement by Hunter et al. (<a id="xref-ref-6-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>) that there is “no reduction in risk even among women whose energy intake
                     
                     was less than 20 percent of total energy intake” does not appear to have been corrected for
                     
                     measurement error. From the results given by Hunter et al. (<a id="xref-ref-6-8" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>), it is not
                     
                     possible to directly estimate what this 20 percent figure translates into in terms of true percent
                     
                     calories from fat. However, it is highly likely that it translates into a figure much greater than
                     
                     20%. From calibration studies we have performed, the average ‘true’
                     
                     percent calories from fat is reasonably close to the average FFQ-derived percent calories from
                     
                     fat. If this is true for the studies analyzed by Hunter et al. (<a id="xref-ref-6-9" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>), then the 20
                     
                     percent figure will translate roughly into (35% −
                     [
                     35%−20%] × 0.28) = 30.8%, where we
                     
                     estimate the average percent calories from fat in the cohort studies as 35%. That is, the
                     
                     statement concerning the ‘20 percent’ figure may be more applicable to a
                     ‘
                     30 percent’ figure, and, in this case, the analytic cohort studies provide no data on
                     
                     the effects of a very low-fat (&lt;20% calories from fat) diet.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-23">The above argument is, of course, hypothetical, since the actual corrected results from the
                     
                     papers included in the meta-analysis by Hunter et al. (<a id="xref-ref-6-10" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-6">6</a>) were not
                     
                     presented. Although we believe our argument is reasonable and the tentative conclusions we
                     
                     have drawn are likely to be true, the actual corrected results from the papers included in the
                     
                     meta-analysis need to be presented.
                  </p>
