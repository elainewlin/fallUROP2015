                  <h2>Abstract</h2>
                  
                  <div id="sec-1" class="subsection">
                     
                     
                     <p id="p-1"><strong>Background</strong> The relationship between active cigarette smoking and breast cancer risk remains controversial because of unresolved issues
                        of confounding and dose response.
                     </p>
                     
                  </div>
                  
                  <div id="sec-2" class="subsection methods">
                     
                     
                     <p id="p-2"><strong>Methods</strong> To investigate these issues further, we analyzed data from 73 388 women in the American Cancer Society’s Cancer Prevention
                        Study II (CPS-II) Nutrition Cohort. Analyses were based on 3721 invasive breast cancer case patients identified during a median
                        follow-up of 13.8 years. Hazard ratios (HRs) and 95% confidence intervals (CIs) were estimated from multivariable-adjusted
                        Cox proportional hazard regression models. <em>P</em> values were two-sided. We also conducted meta-analyses of our results with those published from 14 other cohort studies.
                     </p>
                     
                  </div>
                  
                  <div id="sec-3" class="subsection">
                     
                     
                     <p id="p-3"><strong>Results</strong> In CPS-II, incidence was higher in current (HR = 1.24, 95% CI = 1.07 to 1.42) and former smokers (HR =1.13, 95% CI = 1.06
                        to 1.21) than in never smokers. Women who initiated smoking before menarche (HR = 1.61, 95% CI = 1.10 to 2.34) or after menarche
                        but 11 or more years before first birth (HR = 1.45, 95% CI = 1.21 to 1.74) had higher risk (<em>P</em>
                        <sub>trend</sub> = .03). No relationships were observed with other smoking parameters. Alcohol consumption did not confound associations with
                        smoking status, although neither current nor former smoking were associated with risk among never drinkers (<em>P</em>
                        <sub>interaction</sub> = .11). In meta-analyses, current (HR = 1.12, 95% CI = 1.08 to 1.16) and former smoking (HR = 1.09, 95% CI = 1.04 to 1.15)
                        were weakly associated with risk; a stronger association (HR = 1.21, 95% CI = 1.14 to 1.28) was observed in women who initiated
                        smoking before first birth.
                     </p>
                     
                  </div>
                  
                  <div id="sec-4" class="subsection">
                     
                     
                     <p id="p-4"><strong>Conclusions</strong> These results support the hypothesis that active smoking is associated with increased breast cancer risk for women who initiate
                        smoking before first birth and suggest that smoking might play a role in breast cancer initiation.
                     </p>
                     
                  </div>
                  
               </div>
               <p id="p-5">Approximately 130 epidemiologic studies and seven consensus reports (<a id="xref-ref-1-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-1">1–7</a>) examined the relationship between active cigarette smoking and breast cancer risk. Despite the wealth of data on this issue,
                  there is still no scientific consensus, partly because of concern that the association may be confounded by alcohol consumption
                  (<a id="xref-ref-8-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-8">8</a>) and partly because studies have not consistently shown an increase in risk with either duration or intensity of smoking
                  (<a id="xref-ref-5-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-5">5</a>). The US Surgeon General last reviewed the literature in 2004 and concluded, “The evidence is suggestive of no causal relationship”
                  (<a id="xref-ref-4-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-4">4</a>). The International Agency for Research on Cancer reexamined the evidence available through 2009 and concluded that there
                  was “[l]imited evidence of carcinogenicity” (<a id="xref-ref-5-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-5">5</a>). However, a quantitative review of published data has not been conducted, although a number of studies have been published
                  in the last 10 years with long-term follow-up, large case ascertainments, and prospective collection of active smoking behaviors
                  (<a id="xref-ref-9-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9">9–17</a>).
               </p>
               <p id="p-6" class="indent">The biological data linking active smoking and breast cancer are more conclusive. Tobacco smoke contains at least 20 chemical
                  compounds that induce mammary cancers in rodents (<a id="xref-ref-3-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-3">3</a>,<a id="xref-ref-4-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-4">4</a>,<a id="xref-ref-18-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-18">18</a>). Some of these compounds are lipophilic, and are deposited and stored in breast adipose tissue and later metabolized and
                  activated by mammary epithelial cells (<a id="xref-ref-19-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-19">19–21</a>). Women who smoke have detectable smoking metabolites in their breast fluid (<a id="xref-ref-22-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-22">22</a>,<a id="xref-ref-23-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-23">23</a>) and a higher prevalence of smoking-specific DNA adducts and p53 mutation smoking signatures in breast tissue than do nonsmokers
                  (<a id="xref-ref-24-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-24">24–28</a>). Although it has been hypothesized that the effects of active smoking on tumor initiation may be masked by smoking’s antiestrogenic
                  effects (<a id="xref-ref-29-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-29">29</a>,<a id="xref-ref-30-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-30">30</a>), the latter effects are based on indirect evidence, including observations from some studies that smokers have an earlier
                  age at natural menopause (<a id="xref-ref-29-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-29">29</a>), higher risk of osteoporosis (<a id="xref-ref-31-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-31">31</a>,<a id="xref-ref-32-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-32">32</a>), lower risk of endometrial cancer (<a id="xref-ref-33-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-33">33</a>), and possibly lower postmenopausal mammographic density (<a id="xref-ref-34-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-34">34</a>,<a id="xref-ref-35-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-35">35</a>). However, a large pooled analysis found heavy smokers had higher, not lower, circulating levels of estrogens and androgens
                  (<a id="xref-ref-36-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-36">36</a>). Thus, the proposed antiestrogenic effect of active cigarette smoking remains hypothetical.
               </p>
               <p id="p-7" class="indent">We examined the relationship between active cigarette smoking and invasive breast cancer risk and evaluated this association
                  for confounding by alcohol intake in the American Cancer Society’s Cancer Prevention Study II (CPS-II) Nutrition Cohort. We
                  also conducted meta-analyses of the results from published cohort studies of active smoking and breast cancer incidence.
               </p>
               <div class="section methods" id="sec-5">
                  <div class="section-nav"><a href="#abstract-1" title="Abstract" class="prev-section-link"><span>Previous Section</span></a><a href="#sec-12" title="Results" class="next-section-link"><span>Next Section</span></a></div>
                  
                  <h2>Methods</h2>
                  
                  <div id="sec-6" class="subsection">
                     
                     <h3>Description of Cohort</h3>
                     
                     <p id="p-8">Women in this analysis were drawn from the 97 786 female participants in the CPS-II Nutrition Cohort, a prospective study
                        of cancer incidence and mortality established in 1992 as a subgroup of a larger mortality study initiated in 1982 (<a id="xref-ref-37-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-37">37</a>). At enrollment, the CPS-II Nutrition Cohort members were aged 50–74 years. All participants completed a mailed baseline
                        questionnaire. Follow-up questionnaires were sent to cohort members every two years, starting in 1997, to update exposure
                        information and ascertain newly diagnosed cancer outcomes. The response rate for each of these follow-up questionnaires through
                        2005 was at least 86%. Informed consent for participation was assumed based on completion and return of study questionnaires.
                        The Emory University School of Medicine Institutional Review Board approves all aspects of CPS-II.
                     </p>
                     
                  </div>
                  
                  <div id="sec-7" class="subsection">
                     
                     <h3>Population for Analysis</h3>
                     
                     <p id="p-9">Individuals were excluded from the analytical dataset if they were alive at the time of the first survey mailing yet returned
                        no surveys during follow-up (n = 3116), reported a history of cancer in 1992 (except nonmelanoma skin cancer, n = 13 100),
                        had incomplete data on smoking (n = 4588), or did not complete the follow-up survey in 1997 (n = 3555).
                     </p>
                     
                  </div>
                  
                  <div id="sec-8" class="subsection">
                     
                     <h3>Breast Cancer Case Patients</h3>
                     
                     <p id="p-10">Incident breast cancer diagnoses were self-reported on follow-up questionnaires through June 30, 2007. Invasive breast cancer
                        case patients (International Classification of Diseases, Tenth Revision code: C50) were verified through medical records (n
                        = 2905) or linkage with state registries (n = 684) (<a id="xref-ref-21-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-21">21</a>). A small number of case patients (n = 98) were identified during confirmation of other reported cancer diagnoses. Interval
                        deaths were obtained through the National Death Index. Breast cancer deaths (n = 34) were included if the death certificate
                        listed invasive breast cancer as a cause of death (<a id="xref-ref-38-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-38">38</a>). Clinical characteristics of the tumor were obtained from state registries or abstracted from medical records.
                     </p>
                     
                  </div>
                  
                  <div id="sec-9" class="subsection">
                     
                     <h3>Exposure Data</h3>
                     
                     <p id="p-11">Baseline smoking history was based on enrollment questionnaires for the 1982 CPS-II Baseline Mortality Cohort and the 1992
                        CPS-II Nutrition Cohort and updated in follow-up questionnaires. Baseline smoking status in 1982 was determined based on the
                        response to the question, “Do you now or have you ever smoked cigarettes, at least one a day for one year’s time?” Ever smokers
                        were then asked questions on the average number of cigarettes smoked per day, the age when they started smoking, and the total
                        number of years they smoked. Former smokers were also asked their age of smoking cessation. Responses were used to create
                        analytical smoking variables, and categories were selected a priori based on prior smoking literature and the distribution
                        in our study population.
                     </p>
                     
                  </div>
                  
                  <div id="sec-10" class="subsection">
                     
                     <h3>Statistical Analyses</h3>
                     
                     <p id="p-12">Participants contributed person-time to the analysis until the first censoring event: 1) diagnosed with cancer (including
                        in situ carcinoma of the breast), 2) died before the end of follow-up, 3) failed to complete any survey, or 4) reached the
                        end of follow-up through June 30, 2007. All models were stratified on year of age at enrollment. Extended Cox regression models
                        (<a id="xref-ref-39-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-39">39</a>) were used to calculate multivariable-adjusted hazard ratios (HRs), 95% confidence intervals (CIs), and the Wald <em>P</em> value. To evaluate the overall effect of smoking status (never, former, current smoker), we also estimated the <em>P</em> value for the likelihood ratio test with 2 degrees of freedom. .
                     </p>
                     
                     <p id="p-13" class="indent">Multivariable-adjusted models included age at menarche, age at first birth, menopausal status and age at menopause, alcohol
                        consumption, race, education, body mass index, family history of breast cancer, history of breast cysts, use of postmenopausal
                        hormone therapy, mammogram within 2 years of the questionnaire, and use of oral contraceptives. Smoking status, alcohol status,
                        use of postmenopausal hormones, and use of mammography screening were treated as time-dependent covariables. All Cox models
                        were stratified on year of age at enrollment. The proportional hazards assumption was evaluated by testing for an interaction
                        by time in the model; no violations were observed.
                     </p>
                     
                     <p id="p-14" class="indent">We assessed effect modification by alcohol intake, family history of breast cancer, mammographic screening, body mass index,
                        and postmenopausal hormone use comparing the −2 log likelihood estimates of models with and without the interaction term(s).
                        Associations were evaluated for subgroups defined by estrogen receptor status, histology, and the Surveillance, Epidemiology
                        and End Results (SEER) summary stage using models to predict risk for one subgroup while censoring for the other subgroups.
                        The <em>P</em> value for tumor heterogeneity was estimated comparing the −2 log likelihood of a case-only model with and without smoking
                        status as an explanatory variable (2 degrees of freedom likelihood ratio test). In sensitivity analysis, the influence of
                        changes in smoking patterns before breast cancer diagnosis was examined by excluding case patients that were diagnosed within
                        the first 2 years of follow-up. Reported <em>P</em> values are two-sided and were considered statistically significant if less than 0.05. <em>P</em> values for linear trend were calculated using the appropriate linear contrasts of the model parameters. These analyses were
                        performed with SAS, version 9.2 (SAS Institute Inc., Cary, NC).
                     </p>
                     
                  </div>
                  
                  <div id="sec-11" class="subsection">
                     
                     <h3>Meta-analysis</h3>
                     
                     <p id="p-15">PubMed searches were conducted to identify studies of the association of active cigarette smoking and breast cancer incidence
                        from prospective cohort studies published in English-language peer-reviewed journals as of November 2012 using search terms
                        “smoking and breast cancer,” “tobacco and breast cancer,” and “cigarettes and breast cancer.” Reference lists of original
                        contributions, review articles (<a id="xref-ref-8-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-8">8</a>,<a id="xref-ref-40-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-40">40–43</a>), and consensus reports (<a id="xref-ref-1-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-1">1–7</a>) were also searched to identify additional articles. Thirty-two articles were identified (<a id="xref-ref-9-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9">9–17</a>,<a id="xref-ref-44-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-44">44–66</a>). Articles were excluded if results were based on analyses of breast cancer mortality (n = 7) (<a id="xref-ref-49-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-49">49</a>,<a id="xref-ref-50-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-50">50</a>,<a id="xref-ref-52-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-52">52–54</a>,<a id="xref-ref-62-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-62">62</a>,<a id="xref-ref-66-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-66">66</a>), were updated with longer follow-up time (n = 7) (<a id="xref-ref-51-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-51">51</a>,<a id="xref-ref-56-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-56">56–59</a>,<a id="xref-ref-61-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-61">61</a>,<a id="xref-ref-64-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-64">64</a>), or did not contain sufficient information to estimate the association separately for current and former smoking (n = 4)
                        (<a id="xref-ref-55-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-55">55</a>,<a id="xref-ref-60-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-60">60</a>,<a id="xref-ref-63-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-63">63</a>,<a id="xref-ref-65-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-65">65</a>). Data from 14 other articles (study descriptions and abbreviated names in <a href="http://jnci.oxfordjournals.org/lookup/suppl/doi:10.1093/jnci/djt023/-/DC1">Supplementary Table 1</a>, available online) were combined with data from the CPS-II Nutrition Cohort in meta-analyses of active smoking status (<a id="xref-ref-9-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9">9–17</a>,<a id="xref-ref-44-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-44">44–48</a>), and eight other articles (<a id="xref-ref-9-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9">9–17</a>) plus our own were combined in a meta-analysis of age at smoking initiation and smoking before first birth. Study-specific
                        hazard ratios (95% CIs) were abstracted from studies for active cigarette smoking, age at smoking initiation (youngest age
                        category), and initiation relative to first birth (“yes” or the most extreme category of number of years before first birth)
                        with nonsmokers as the referent category. Hazard ratios for smoking status from two studies were calculated using published
                        hazard ratios for current smokers from categories of packs per day (<a id="xref-ref-44-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-44">44</a>) and for current and former smokers from premenopausal and postmenopausal estimates (<a id="xref-ref-46-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-46">46</a>) using a fixed-effects model (<a id="xref-ref-67-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-67">67</a>). Five studies (<a id="xref-ref-13-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-13">13</a>,<a id="xref-ref-44-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-44">44–46</a>,<a id="xref-ref-68-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-68">68</a>) did not examine timing of smoking initiation and therefore were not included in the meta-analysis; the Swedish Population
                        Registry cohort only examined age at smoking initiation (<a id="xref-ref-48-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-48">48</a>). Summary hazard ratio (95% CI) estimates were calculated using a random-effects model, with each study result weighted by
                        the within- and between-study variances (<a id="xref-ref-67-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-67">67</a>). Between-study heterogeneity was assessed by the Conchran <em>Q</em> test (<a id="xref-ref-67-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-67">67</a>) and the <em>I</em>
                        <sup>2</sup> (the proportion of variation in the relative risks attributable to heterogeneity). Publication bias was assessed by Begg
                        test (<a id="xref-ref-69-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-69">69</a>). The meta-analyses were performed in STATA SE (version 11.0).
                     </p>
                     
                  </div>

                  <div id="sec-13" class="subsection">
                     
                     <h3>Baseline Characteristics of CPS-II Nutrition Cohort</h3>
                     
                     <p id="p-16">During a median follow-up of 13.8 years, 3721 invasive breast cancer case patients were identified among the 73 388 women
                        in the analytic cohort. At enrollment, 8.2% of women reported current smoking, 35.6% reported former smoking, and 56.2% reported
                        never smoking (<a id="xref-table-wrap-1-1" class="xref-table" href="#T1">Table 1</a>). The age-standardized incidence rates of breast cancer were 474 per 100 000 person-years among current smokers, 485 per
                        100 000 person-years among former smokers, and 411 per 100 000 person-years among never smokers. On average, current smokers
                        smoked more than twice as many cigarettes per day for twice as long as former smokers. The median age of smoking initiation
                        was similar for both ever smoker groups. Current smokers were less likely to be overweight or obese, have a child at age 30
                        years or older, experience menopause at age 55 or older, use postmenopausal hormones, or to have received a recent mammogram
                        compared with both never and former smokers. Both current and former smokers were more likely to consume alcohol and to use
                        oral contraceptives compared with nonsmokers (<a id="xref-table-wrap-1-2" class="xref-table" href="#T1">Table 1</a>).

                  <h2>Discussion</h2>
                  
                  <p id="p-39">In the CPS-II Nutrition Cohort, a prospective study of more than 73 000 predominantly postmenopausal women, the incidence
                     of invasive breast cancer was higher in current or former smokers than in never smokers. The risk was highest in women who
                     initiated smoking at an early age during the period of biological vulnerability before age at first birth. Alcohol consumption
                     did not appreciably confound these associations. However, the elevated risk was confined to current or former alcohol drinkers,
                     although the confidence intervals were wide for never drinkers. Meta-analysis of the CPS-II results together with those from
                     14 published cohort studies (<a id="xref-ref-9-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9">9–17</a>,<a id="xref-ref-44-5" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-44">44–48</a>) found similar associations between breast cancer risk and smoking status, with a linear positive association between years
                     of smoking before first pregnancy and risk.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-40" class="indent">In the CPS-II Nutrition Cohort, the relationship between active smoking and breast cancer incidence differs from that observed
                     with other smoking-related cancers in that there are no dose–response relationships with overall longer duration of smoking,
                     greater number of cigarettes smoked per day, or years since quitting. Most of the current smokers in our study had smoked
                     for at least 20 years before enrollment, precluding analyses of short-term current smokers. Similarly, other cohort studies
                     observed little or no linear dose–response relationships for either duration or intensity of smoking or number of years since
                     cessation (<a id="xref-ref-9-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9">9–17</a>,<a id="xref-ref-44-6" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-44">44–48</a>).
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-41" class="indent">The most consistent evidence supporting a causal relationship between cigarette smoking and breast cancer risk is the stronger
                     association observed for women who initiate smoking before age at first birth. Mammary tissue is thought to be more susceptible
                     to genotoxic exposures before completion of the first full-term pregnancy (<a id="xref-ref-70-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-70">70</a>) because the terminal ductal–lobular units of the breast are not fully differentiated until the end of gestation (<a id="xref-ref-70-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-70">70–73</a>). The relationship with early life smoking that we and others (<a id="xref-ref-9-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9">9–17</a>,<a id="xref-ref-48-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-48">48</a>) observed, together with the lack of a consistent relationship between breast cancer risk and smoking later in life, suggests
                     that active cigarette smoking may play a greater role in the initiation than the progression of breast cancer.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-42" class="indent">Although the association between active smoking and breast cancer is considerably weaker than that of many other smoking-related
                     cancers (<a id="xref-ref-5-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-5">5</a>), the relationship is reasonably consistent in well-conducted cohort studies with long follow-up. All but three of the studies
                     included in the meta-analysis (<a id="xref-ref-44-7" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-44">44</a>,<a id="xref-ref-45-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-45">45</a>,<a id="xref-ref-48-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-48">48</a>) reported multivariable-adjusted hazard ratios for current smokers above the null; in four studies (<a id="xref-ref-10-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10">10</a>,<a id="xref-ref-14-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-14">14</a>,<a id="xref-ref-16-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-16">16</a>,<a id="xref-ref-17-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-17">17</a>) and in ours, the association was statistically significant. The summary estimate for current smoking of 1.12 was included
                     in the confidence intervals of all 15 studies. Associations for former smoking were weaker but still positive in 13 of 15
                     studies (<a id="xref-ref-9-8" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-9">9–17</a>,<a id="xref-ref-44-8" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-44">44–48</a>), excluding only the Canadian National Breast Screening Study (<a id="xref-ref-10-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10">10</a>) and the Members of a Large Prepaid Health Plan in Northern California (Kaiser2) (<a id="xref-ref-44-9" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-44">44</a>) cohorts, which found no association and an inverse association, respectively, with former smoking (<a id="xref-ref-10-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10">10</a>).
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-43" class="indent">A persistent question in the breast cancer/smoking controversy concerns potential confounding by alcohol consumption. Alcohol
                     consumption, even at low to moderate levels (<a id="xref-ref-5-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-5">5</a>,<a id="xref-ref-74-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-74">74</a>,<a id="xref-ref-75-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-75">75</a>), is a known risk factor for breast cancer. Alcohol consumption also is correlated with cigarette smoking (<a id="xref-ref-76-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-76">76</a>), and current smokers, on average, drink more than former or never smokers (<a id="xref-ref-76-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-76">76</a>). However, in our study, the association between active smoking and breast cancer incidence was not attenuated by the inclusion
                     of alcohol intake in the multivariable model. The association was statistically significant only in current and former drinkers
                     in analyses that stratified on alcohol consumption, although the confidence intervals for the association in never drinkers
                     were wide. Unfortunately, only two other cohort studies (<a id="xref-ref-10-4" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-10">10</a>,<a id="xref-ref-11-1" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-11">11</a>) examined interactions with alcohol intake; neither study reported a statistically significant interaction. However, a recent
                     report on smoking and mortality from the Million Women Study found the association with breast cancer mortality was attenuated
                     from 1.13 to 1.06 (95% CI = 0.95 to 1.18) among women who consumed little to no alcohol (<a id="xref-ref-66-2" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-66">66</a>). Given the modest association observed between active smoking and breast cancer incidence and the emphasis on potential
                     confounding by alcohol consumption in the former meta-analysis of 53 case–control and cohort studies (<a id="xref-ref-8-3" class="xref-bibr" href="#ref-8">8</a>), it is important that this issue be examined further.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-44" class="indent">The CPS-II cohort provides long-term follow-up of birth cohorts of women who began smoking after World War II. The weight
                     of our conclusions are strengthened by the study’s prospective design, large sample size, and detailed information on smoking
                     habits and covariables known to be associated with breast cancer.
                  </p>
                  
                  <p id="p-45" class="indent">Our study was not without limitations. We were unable to control for exposure to secondhand smoke in the CPS-II cohort.</p>
                  
                  <p id="p-46" class="indent">In summary, the findings in our cohort and in the meta-analysis of prospective studies support the hypothesis that active
                     smoking increases breast cancer risk, especially when smoking begins at an early age.
                  </p>
